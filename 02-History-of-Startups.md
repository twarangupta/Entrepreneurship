> *Part 1 — Startup Foundations*

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

1. Explain why the modern startup is a relatively *recent* invention, not an eternal feature of business.
2. Identify the three ingredients that had to come together for startups to exist: a **scalable technology**, a **funding model for risk**, and a **falling cost of starting**.
3. Trace the major waves of technology — transistor, integrated circuit, minicomputer, personal computer, internet, mobile, cloud, AI — and explain how each created a new generation of startups.
4. Understand where **venture capital** came from and why it was a genuine financial innovation.
5. Explain what the **dot-com bubble** was, why it inflated, why it burst, and what survived.
6. Understand why the cost of starting a startup collapsed after ~2005, and why that changed everything.
7. Recognize the recurring **pattern** behind every wave — and use it to think about the wave you are living in now.

## Introduction

In Chapter 1 we defined a startup as a new company designed to grow fast, powered by a business model with low marginal cost. A natural question follows: *if this is such a powerful idea, why is it so recent?*

Humans have run businesses for thousands of years. Merchants, craftsmen, traders, banks, trading companies — commerce is ancient. Yet the *startup*, in the specific sense we mean, is barely seventy years old, and the version most of this book describes is younger still — roughly two decades. A founder in the year 1850 could build a great business, but could not build a "startup" the way a teenager with a laptop can today. Something had to change in the world first.

This chapter explains what changed. Understanding the history is not trivia. It reveals the *machinery* that makes startups possible — and that machinery is still running, in new forms, right now. Every wave of startups has followed the same underlying pattern, and once you see the pattern, you can recognize the opening of a new wave while it's happening, instead of reading about it ten years later.

We'll move chronologically, but always asking *why* — why this became possible *now*, and what it teaches a founder today.

## Beginner Explanation

Let's start with the simplest possible version of the whole story.

### Three things had to exist before startups could

For a startup — a company that grows huge, fast, from almost nothing — to be possible at all, three ingredients had to come together:

1. **A technology that scales cheaply.** Remember the snowball from Chapter 1. You need a way to serve *many more* people without your costs growing just as fast. Before machines and software, almost everything required human hands and physical materials, so serving more customers always cost proportionally more. There was no snowball to roll.

2. **A way to fund risky bets.** Startups usually lose money for years while they search for what works, and most fail. A normal bank will not lend money to something that will probably fail and has no physical assets to seize if it does. So a *new kind of money* had to be invented — money that expects most bets to fail but bets anyway, because the rare winner pays for everything. That invention is called **venture capital**.

3. **A low cost of starting.** If building the first version of a product costs millions, only the wealthy or well-connected can even try. The more it costs to *start*, the fewer people can experiment, and the fewer startups exist. As starting got cheaper and cheaper, more people could try, and the number of startups exploded.

> **Venture capital (VC)** — money invested in young, high-risk companies in exchange for part-ownership (equity), in the expectation that most will fail but a few will succeed so spectacularly that they more than make up for the losses. We'll study how VC actually works in Part 7; here we just need to know it exists and why it was necessary.

### The simple story in one paragraph

For most of history, the first two ingredients didn't exist, so there were no startups — only craftsmen and merchants whose growth was limited by their hands. In the mid-1900s, **electronics** (transistors, then chips, then computers) gave the world a technology that could scale, and around the same time **venture capital** was invented to fund the risky companies built on it. For decades, startups existed but were expensive and rare. Then, around 2005, the **internet plus cheap "cloud" computing** made starting a software company almost free — and the number of startups, and the speed at which they could grow, exploded. The smartphone (2007) and, most recently, artificial intelligence have each opened brand-new waves. That, in one paragraph, is the history of startups.

```text
THE THREE INGREDIENTS (all three required)

   ┌─────────────────────┐
   │ Scalable technology │  ── lets one company serve millions cheaply
   └─────────────────────┘
   ┌─────────────────────┐
   │ Funding for risk    │  ── venture capital: bets on the rare giant
   │ (venture capital)   │
   └─────────────────────┘
   ┌─────────────────────┐
   │ Low cost to start   │  ── lets many people try → more shots on goal
   └─────────────────────┘
            │
            ▼
     The modern startup
```

## Intermediate Explanation

Now let's walk through the actual waves of history, decade by decade, and watch the three ingredients assemble and then improve.

### The deep past: why there were no startups

Before industrialization, business growth was bounded by physical limits — land, raw materials, and human labor. A weaver could weave only so much cloth; to weave more, you needed more weavers. Costs rose in step with output. In Chapter 1's language, *every* business was a "wall," not a "snowball." There was no engine for explosive, low-cost growth, so there was nothing for risk-money to chase, so neither scalable technology nor venture capital existed. The conditions for a startup simply weren't there.

The Industrial Revolution (roughly the 1800s) changed the ceiling — factories and machines let output grow faster than labor — but starting a factory required enormous capital, land, and machinery. You could build large industrial companies, but only with large sums and physical scale. This is the world of railroads, steel, and oil: big, capital-heavy, and funded by wealthy financiers and banks, not anything resembling a startup ecosystem.

### 1947–1959: the transistor and the first real scalable technology

The turning point was electronics. In 1947, researchers at **Bell Labs** invented the **transistor** — a tiny switch with no moving parts that could be made smaller, cheaper, and more reliable over time. This mattered enormously, because for the first time there was a technology whose cost per unit of capability *fell* relentlessly as it improved. That falling-cost property is the seed of every snowball that followed.

In 1957, a group of eight engineers (later nicknamed the **"traitorous eight"**) left a difficult boss, William Shockley, and founded **Fairchild Semiconductor** in what would later be called Silicon Valley. Fairchild became the ancestor of an entire industry. The companies it spun off — the **"Fairchildren"** — included, most famously, **Intel** (founded 1968 by Robert Noyce and Gordon Moore).

This period also gave us one of the most important observations in all of technology, **Moore's Law**: Gordon Moore noticed in 1965 that the number of transistors that could be packed onto a chip was roughly doubling every couple of years — meaning computing power got cheaper and cheaper, predictably, for decades. Moore's Law is, in a sense, the engine that powered wave after wave of startups: each year, the same money bought far more computing, so things that were impossible or unaffordable one year became cheap a few years later.

> **Moore's Law (simplified)** — the observation that computing power per dollar roughly doubles every ~two years. It isn't a law of physics; it's a long-running trend. Its importance to startups: it guarantees that *next year, more things become cheap enough to build a business on.*

### 1946–1972: the invention of venture capital

A scalable technology was now appearing — but who would fund the risky young companies built on it? Banks wouldn't. So the second ingredient had to be invented.

In 1946, **Georges Doriot**, a Harvard Business School professor, helped create **American Research and Development Corporation (ARDC)** — widely considered the first true venture capital firm. ARDC's most famous bet: in 1957 it invested about **$70,000** in a young computer company, **Digital Equipment Corporation (DEC)**. When DEC later went public, that stake was worth hundreds of millions — a single investment that returned the entire fund many times over. This was the proof of the venture model's core math: *one giant winner outweighs many losers.* We'll formalize this "power law" in Part 7.

In the late 1950s and 1960s, financiers like **Arthur Rock** refined the model and helped fund Fairchild and later Intel and Apple. The crucial structural innovation was the **limited partnership** — a way to pool money from large investors (pension funds, universities, wealthy individuals) into a fund run by professional investors, who would spread it across many risky startups. By the early 1970s, the now-legendary firms **Kleiner Perkins** and **Sequoia Capital** were founded (both 1972), clustering along **Sand Hill Road** near Stanford. The funding machine for startups now existed.

```text
WHY VENTURE CAPITAL HAD TO BE INVENTED

  A bank lends $1M, expects $1M back + interest, hates risk.
  → Useless for startups (most fail, no assets to seize).

  A VC invests in 10 startups:
     7 fail        → lose everything
     2 do okay     → return the money
     1 is a giant  → returns 50× and pays for everything

  Total result: a big profit, despite mostly failing.
  This math only works with a special kind of money.
```

### 1968–1984: minicomputers and the personal computer

With both scalable technology and venture funding in place, the first real startup waves arrived.

First came **minicomputers** — smaller, cheaper computers (like DEC's) that more organizations could afford. Then, as Moore's Law made chips cheap enough, came the truly revolutionary idea: a computer for *one person*. Hobbyists in groups like the **Homebrew Computer Club** tinkered with early kits. Out of that culture, in 1976, **Steve Jobs** and **Steve Wozniak** founded **Apple**, funded early by investor **Mike Markkula**. In 1975, **Bill Gates** and **Paul Allen** had founded **Microsoft** to write software for these new machines.

The **personal computer (PC)** was a perfect startup substrate: it was a *platform* — a base that other companies could build products on top of. Microsoft didn't make the IBM PC; it made the software that ran on it, and that software business scaled with near-zero marginal cost (copy a floppy disk; sell it again). This is the first appearance, at massive scale, of the software economics from Chapter 1.

### 1989–2000: the internet and the dot-com boom

The next platform was the biggest yet: the **internet**, and specifically the **World Wide Web**, invented by **Tim Berners-Lee** around 1989–1991. In 1993, the **Mosaic** browser made the web usable by ordinary people; in 1994, **Netscape** turned that into a product, and its blockbuster **1995 IPO** is often treated as the starting gun of the dot-com boom.

The web was an extraordinary startup substrate because it added *global distribution* to cheap software. Now a small company could reach the entire connected world instantly, at almost no cost per user. The snowball could roll across the planet. Landmark companies were founded in a rush: **Amazon** (1994), **eBay** (1995), **Google** (1998), and countless others.

But the same excitement produced the first great cautionary tale, which we treat as a case study below: the **dot-com bubble**. We'll return to it shortly.

## Advanced Explanation

Now the deeper mechanics — the parts that matter most for thinking like a founder today.

### The 2005 inflection: when starting a startup became almost free

The single most important change for the *modern* startup — the world this book is mostly about — happened in the mid-2000s, and it was about **cost**.

Before ~2000, to launch an internet company you had to buy physical servers, rent space for them, buy expensive enterprise software licenses, and hire specialists to run it all. The upfront cost ran into the hundreds of thousands or millions of dollars. This is why the third ingredient — *low cost of starting* — was still missing, and why startups remained relatively rare and dependent on big upfront funding.

Then three things collapsed that cost almost simultaneously:

1. **Open-source software.** Free, high-quality software (the Linux operating system, web servers, databases, programming languages and frameworks) meant you no longer had to buy expensive licenses. The tools became free.

2. **Cloud computing.** In 2006, **Amazon Web Services (AWS)** let anyone *rent* computing power by the hour instead of buying servers. Suddenly you didn't need to spend money on infrastructure until you actually had users — and your costs grew only as your usage grew. This is the single biggest reason marginal costs fell so far for so many startups.

3. **The standardized seed model.** In 2005, **Y Combinator** (founded by Paul Graham, Jessica Livingston, Robert Morris, and Trevor Blackwell) pioneered a new way to fund the *earliest* stage: give many tiny companies a small, standardized amount of money and intense mentorship in batches. This made very-early funding cheap, fast, and repeatable, dramatically increasing the number of startups that could get their first dollars.

The combined effect was historic. The cost to build and launch an internet startup fell from roughly *millions* to roughly *thousands* (sometimes effectively zero) in just a few years.

```text
COST TO LAUNCH AN INTERNET STARTUP (rough, illustrative)

  ~1999:  $$$$$$$$$$$$$$$$$$$$  (millions — buy servers, licenses, staff)
  ~2005:  $$$$$                 (tens of thousands)
  ~2010:  $$                    (thousands — AWS + open source)
  Today:  $                     (often near zero to start)
```

Why does this matter so much? Because the number of startups — and therefore the number of *shots on goal* at finding the rare giant winner — is governed largely by the cost of starting. Cut the cost by 100×, and vastly more people can try, from vastly more places, with vastly more diverse ideas. The explosion of startups after 2005 was not mainly because people suddenly got more ambitious; it was because the *barrier to trying* fell through the floor.

### The mobile wave: a brand-new platform from scratch (2007–)

Just as the web wave matured, a new platform appeared. Apple's **iPhone** (2007) and its **App Store** (2008) created an entirely new substrate: a powerful, internet-connected computer in everyone's pocket, with a built-in distribution channel (the app store) and brand-new capabilities (location, camera, always-on, push notifications).

A new platform means new things become possible that weren't before — and *that* is where new giant startups come from. **Uber** needed everyone to have a GPS-equipped phone. **Instagram** needed a good camera in every pocket. **WhatsApp** needed cheap mobile data. None of these could have existed on the 1999 web. The lesson, which we'll formalize, is that **each new platform unlocks a new generation of startups that were previously impossible** — and the founders who recognize the new platform early have an enormous advantage.

### The SaaS and lean-startup eras run in parallel

Two other threads were weaving through this period:

- **SaaS (Software as a Service).** Instead of selling software in a box that customers install, you run it centrally and let customers use it over the internet for a recurring subscription. **Salesforce** (founded 1999, famous for its "No Software" slogan) pioneered this at scale. SaaS turned software into a predictable, recurring-revenue snowball — so important that all of Part 4 is devoted to it.

- **The Lean Startup movement.** As starting got cheap, a new *methodology* emerged for how to search efficiently. **Steve Blank** (*The Four Steps to the Epiphany*, 2005) reframed the startup as a search (the definition we used in Chapter 1), and his student **Eric Ries** (*The Lean Startup*, 2011) popularized disciplined, fast, cheap experimentation — build a small thing, measure how people respond, learn, repeat. This is the intellectual backbone of Part 3.

### Money cycles: booms, busts, and "cheap money"

One more advanced theme: the *amount* of money flowing into startups rises and falls in cycles, and these cycles shape what gets built.

When money is cheap and plentiful (for example, the low-interest-rate years of much of the 2010s, peaking around 2021), investors pour money into startups, valuations rise, and growth is rewarded over profit. When money tightens (for example, when interest rates rose sharply in 2022), funding contracts, valuations fall, and investors suddenly demand efficiency and profitability instead of growth-at-all-costs.

A founder needs to understand which environment they're raising into, because the *same business* is judged by different standards in different years. We'll cover this properly in Part 7, but the historical point is simple: **the startup world breathes in and out**, and these macroeconomic tides are as much a part of its history as the technology waves.

### The recurring pattern (the most important takeaway in the chapter)

Step back from the details and a single pattern repeats across every wave:

```text
THE STARTUP WAVE PATTERN (repeats every generation)

  1. A new PLATFORM appears
     (mainframe → minicomputer → PC → web → mobile → cloud → AI)
            │
            ▼
  2. It makes new things POSSIBLE & CHEAP
     that were impossible or unaffordable before
            │
            ▼
  3. A wave of STARTUPS rushes to build on it
     (most fail; a few become giants)
            │
            ▼
  4. The platform MATURES; the easy wins are taken
            │
            ▼
  5. A NEW platform appears → the cycle restarts
```

Every giant startup you can name rode a platform shift at roughly the right moment: Microsoft on the PC, Amazon and Google on the web, Uber and Instagram on mobile, the current generation on cloud and AI. The platform doesn't guarantee success — most companies in each wave still fail — but it creates the *opening*. Founders who understand this watch for the next platform the way surfers watch for the next wave.

### Where we are now: the AI wave

The most recent platform shift is **artificial intelligence**. **OpenAI** (founded 2015) released **ChatGPT** in late 2022, and the rapid improvement of AI models since then has opened a new wave with the same structure as every previous one: a new platform makes new things possible and cheap, and a rush of startups races to build on it. As with every wave, most will fail, the hype will outrun reality for a while (as it did in the dot-com boom), and a small number will become the defining companies of the era. You are reading this book during the opening years of that wave — which is precisely why understanding the *pattern*, not just the current buzzwords, is so valuable.

## Why It Matters

History can feel optional next to practical skills. It isn't, for three concrete reasons:

- **It teaches you to spot openings.** The single biggest factor in many startup successes is *timing* — building the right thing just as a new platform makes it possible. (Timing gets its own chapter in Part 2.) You cannot recognize a timing window if you don't understand the wave pattern that creates them.

- **It inoculates you against hype cycles.** Every wave inflates a bubble of overexcitement, then crashes, then quietly produces its real giants afterward. A founder who knows this stays calm: not seduced by the mania, not despairing in the crash, focused on building something real that survives both.

- **It explains the rules you'll have to live by.** Why do investors fund mostly-failing portfolios? Why does cheap money one year and tight money the next change how you should run your company? These rules aren't arbitrary; they're the product of the history in this chapter, and knowing the *why* lets you work with them instead of being surprised by them.

## Real-World Examples

### Case study 1 — Digital Equipment Corporation (DEC): the bet that proved venture capital works

**Situation.** In 1957, computers were room-sized machines sold only by giants like IBM. A young company, DEC, proposed building smaller, cheaper "minicomputers." No bank would fund such an unproven idea.

**The decision.** ARDC, the first venture firm, invested roughly $70,000 for a large ownership stake — a risky bet on an untested market.

**Why it worked.** Minicomputers found a huge market, DEC grew enormously, and ARDC's small stake became worth hundreds of millions. This one outcome *demonstrated the entire venture model*: a single giant winner more than paid for every losing bet.

**The lesson.** The power of startups isn't that they usually succeed — they usually don't. It's that the rare success can be so large that it justifies a whole portfolio of failures. This "power law" math (Part 7) underlies the entire industry, and DEC was its first dramatic proof.

### Case study 2 — The dot-com bubble: what happens when hype outruns reality

**Situation.** From roughly 1995 to early 2000, excitement about the internet drove investors to fund almost any company with ".com" in its name, often regardless of whether it had a workable business model. Stock valuations soared far beyond what the underlying businesses could justify.

**The decision (collectively).** Investors and founders prioritized *growth and land-grab* over the basic economics from Chapter 1 — ignoring marginal costs, unit economics, and whether customers actually paid enough to cover the cost of serving them.

**Why it burst.** Starting in 2000, the realization spread that many of these companies could never make money. The bubble collapsed; trillions in market value evaporated; thousands of startups died. **Pets.com** (selling pet supplies online with shipping costs that swallowed its margins) and **Webvan** (online groceries that scaled physical warehouses before proving the model — classic *premature scaling*) became the symbols of the crash.

**The lesson — and the twist.** The crash did *not* mean the internet was a fad. The web was real and transformative — but the *timing and economics* of many specific companies were wrong. Crucially, the survivors and the companies founded *after* the crash (Amazon survived; Google, founded 1998, thrived) became the true giants. **A real platform shift produces both a bubble of doomed companies and a small number of enduring giants. The skill is telling them apart — which comes down to the fundamentals from Chapter 1: real demand and real economics.** Watch for this exact pattern in the current AI wave.

### Case study 3 — AWS: the invisible startup that created a million startups

**Situation.** In the early 2000s, every internet startup wasted enormous time and money buying and running its own servers before it could even test an idea.

**The decision.** Amazon, having built large-scale infrastructure for itself, launched **Amazon Web Services (2006)** to *rent* that infrastructure to anyone, by the hour.

**Why it mattered.** This single product collapsed the cost of starting a software company. Founders could now launch with almost no upfront money and pay only as they grew. The flood of startups in the following decade — and the entire "two people and a laptop" startup culture — was largely enabled by this shift.

**The lesson.** The most consequential startups sometimes aren't the famous consumer apps but the *platforms and tools that lower the cost of building everything else*. As a founder, "sell shovels to the gold miners" — building the infrastructure a new wave needs — is often a more durable opportunity than chasing the gold yourself.

## Common Mistakes

Mistakes that come from ignoring history:

1. **Assuming startups are timeless and the rules are fixed.** They aren't. The cost of starting, the availability of funding, and the dominant platform all change. Advice from a different era can be actively wrong today.

2. **Mistaking a hype bubble for the real opportunity.** In every wave, excitement runs ahead of reality. Founders who chase the hype (rather than real demand and real economics) get crushed when the bubble pops.

3. **Mistaking a real opportunity for *just* hype.** The opposite error. After a crash, many conclude "the whole thing was a fad" — and miss that the real giants are built *afterward*. The web was real; so, very likely, is AI.

4. **Ignoring the funding climate.** Running a "grow at all costs" playbook in a tight-money year (or hoarding cash timidly in a boom) means fighting the tide instead of riding it.

5. **Missing the next platform.** Building yet another company for *yesterday's* platform, where the easy wins are gone, instead of looking for where a new platform has just opened fresh, unclaimed opportunities.

## Investor Perspective

Investors are, in a real sense, students of this history — because their whole job is to spot the *next* wave early and fund the companies that will ride it. Several investor instincts come straight from the history in this chapter:

- **Platform shifts are where giants come from.** Experienced investors actively hunt for the moment a new platform (mobile in 2008, AI now) opens, because that's when the next generation of huge companies is born. A startup riding a fresh platform shift is far more interesting to them than one fighting for scraps on a mature one.

- **Bubbles are expected, and survival matters more than peak valuation.** Investors who lived through the dot-com crash know that valuations in a hot wave are often detached from reality, and that the prize goes to companies built to *survive the inevitable correction*. This is why even in boom times the best investors probe your fundamental economics.

- **The power law governs everything.** The DEC lesson — one giant winner pays for all the losers — is the mathematical heart of the venture model and shapes every decision an investor makes (Part 7). It's why they push portfolio companies toward enormous outcomes rather than safe, modest ones.

---

## Founder Perspective

For a founder, the history converts into a small number of practical instincts:

- **Ask what's newly possible.** The most fertile founder question isn't "what problem can I solve?" in the abstract, but "what just became *possible* or *cheap* that wasn't a few years ago?" New platforms create those openings, and they're where outsized opportunities hide.

- **Respect the fundamentals through every hype cycle.** Hype will tempt you to skip the boring questions — real demand, real unit economics — that the dot-com casualties skipped. Don't. The companies that survive every wave are the ones that never abandoned Chapter 1's snowball test.

- **Use the falling cost of starting.** You are alive at the cheapest moment in history to start a company. The barriers that stopped earlier generations — buying servers, raising millions just to begin — are largely gone. This is an enormous gift; use it by *starting and testing cheaply* (Part 3) rather than waiting for permission or a big round of funding.

- **Know which way the money tide is flowing.** Before you raise (Part 7), understand whether you're in a boom or a bust, because it changes what investors want and how you should run the company.

## Practical Exercises

1. **Trace a company to its wave.** Pick three companies you use daily. For each, identify which platform wave it rode and *what newly became possible* that allowed it to exist. (Example: WhatsApp → mobile wave → cheap mobile data + phones in every pocket.)

2. **Find the missing ingredient.** Think of a startup idea that would have been impossible 15 years ago. Which of the three ingredients (scalable tech, funding, low starting cost) — or which new platform capability — makes it possible *now*?

3. **Bubble or real?** Choose one heavily hyped category in the current AI wave. Apply Chapter 1's snowball test and ask the dot-com question: *does this have real demand and real economics, or is it riding hype?* Write a short verdict. (You're practicing the single most valuable judgment in a wave.)

4. **The cheap-start audit.** List everything you would need to launch a simple software product today. Estimate the real cost. Notice how small it is — and let that sink in as motivation to *start cheaply* rather than wait.

5. **Spot the next platform.** Speculate, in a paragraph, about what the *next* platform after AI might be, and what it would make newly possible. There's no right answer; the exercise trains the wave-spotting instinct.

7. New platform appears → makes new things possible and cheap → a rush of startups builds on it (most fail, a few become giants) → the platform matures and easy wins are taken → a new platform appears and the cycle repeats.
8. Because the *same business* is judged differently in different climates: cheap money rewards growth-at-all-costs; tight money demands efficiency and profitability. Knowing which environment you're in lets you run and fund the company appropriately (Part 7).
</details>

## Summary

The modern startup is a recent invention because it required three ingredients that didn't always exist: a **scalable technology** (so one company can serve millions cheaply — the snowball), a **funding model for risk** (venture capital, which bets on mostly-failing portfolios because the rare giant pays for all the losses), and a **low cost of starting** (so many people can try).

These assembled over decades. The **transistor** (1947) and **Moore's Law** gave the world a relentlessly cheapening, scalable technology. **Venture capital** was invented (ARDC's DEC bet proved the model) and structured into the modern fund. Wave after wave of platforms followed — the **personal computer** (Microsoft, Apple), the **web** (Amazon, Google), each opening a new generation of startups. The web wave also produced the first great cautionary tale, the **dot-com bubble**, whose lesson endures: real platform shifts produce both doomed hype and lasting giants, and the difference is real demand and real economics.

The decisive change for the *modern* startup came around **2005–2010**, when open-source software, **cloud computing (AWS)**, and standardized seed funding (**Y Combinator**) collapsed the cost of starting from millions to near zero — multiplying the number of people who could try. The **mobile** wave (iPhone, 2007) then showed, again, that each new platform unlocks previously impossible companies (Uber, Instagram). Today, the **AI** wave is repeating the same pattern in real time.

Underneath all of it runs a single repeating cycle: a new platform appears, makes new things possible and cheap, draws a rush of startups (most failing, a few becoming giants), matures, and is replaced by the next platform. Learning to see that pattern — and to keep applying Chapter 1's fundamentals through every hype cycle — is the practical gift this history gives a founder.

## Key Takeaways

- The modern startup needed three ingredients: **scalable technology, venture capital, and a low cost of starting**.
- **Venture capital** exists because startups mostly fail and banks won't fund them; it relies on rare giant winners (the "power law," Part 7).
- **Moore's Law** keeps making new things cheap enough to build on, fueling successive waves.
- Around **2005–2010**, open source + **cloud (AWS)** + standardized seed funding (**Y Combinator**) collapsed the cost of starting — the change that created today's startup world.
- **Each new platform** (PC → web → mobile → cloud → AI) **unlocks a new generation of startups.** Spotting the new platform early is a huge advantage.
- The **dot-com bubble** teaches that real waves produce both hype-driven failures and enduring giants; tell them apart with **real demand and real economics**.
- The startup world moves in **funding cycles** (cheap money vs. tight money); know which you're in.
- There is a **repeating wave pattern**; recognizing it lets you see openings while they're happening.
- We are in the **opening years of the AI wave** — the same pattern, playing out now.

## Glossary of New Terms

- **Venture capital (VC)** — Money invested in young, high-risk companies for equity, expecting most to fail but a rare few to succeed enormously.
- **Transistor** — A tiny electronic switch (invented 1947) whose cost-per-capability falls over time; the foundation of all modern computing.
- **Moore's Law** — The long-running trend that computing power per dollar roughly doubles every ~two years.
- **Integrated circuit / chip** — Many transistors combined on one piece of silicon; the building block of computers.
- **Platform** — A foundation (PC, web, smartphone, cloud, AI) that other companies build products on top of; new platforms unlock new startups.
- **Limited partnership** — The legal structure that pools money from large investors into a fund run by professional venture investors (detailed in Part 7).
- **IPO (Initial Public Offering)** — When a company first sells shares to the public on a stock market (detailed in Part 7); historically a key way investors realized returns.
- **Dot-com bubble** — The late-1990s surge and 2000 crash of internet-company valuations driven by hype outrunning real economics.
- **Open-source software** — Software that is free to use and modify; collapsed the tooling cost of starting a company.
- **Cloud computing** — Renting computing power and storage over the internet (e.g., AWS) instead of buying your own; collapsed the infrastructure cost of starting.
- **AWS (Amazon Web Services)** — Amazon's cloud platform (launched 2006) that let startups rent infrastructure by the hour.
- **Y Combinator (YC)** — The accelerator (founded 2005) that standardized early-stage seed funding and mentorship in batches.
- **SaaS (Software as a Service)** — Software delivered over the internet for a recurring subscription rather than sold as a one-time installed product (Part 4).
- **Lean Startup** — A methodology (Blank, Ries) of cheap, fast experimentation to search efficiently for a working model (Part 3).
- **Power law** — The pattern where a tiny number of outcomes (giant winners) dominate the total result; the mathematical heart of venture investing (Part 7).
- **Funding cycle** — The boom-and-bust rhythm of how much money flows into startups, driven partly by interest rates and the broader economy.
