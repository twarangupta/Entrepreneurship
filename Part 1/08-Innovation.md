# Chapter 8 — Innovation

> *Part 1 — Startup Foundations*

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

1. Distinguish an **invention** from an **innovation** — and explain why "new" is not the same as "valuable."
2. Identify *where* innovation can happen — not only in technology, but in the **product, the business model, and the process**.
3. Explain why **business-model innovation** is often more powerful than technological innovation, with examples.
4. Understand the **diffusion of innovations** (the adoption curve) and the dangerous gap within it called **the chasm**.
5. Apply the **10x rule**: why a new thing usually must be *dramatically* better to displace what already exists.
6. Recognize the **novelty trap** — "technology in search of a problem" — and avoid building clever things nobody wants.
7. Understand innovation as a **repeatable process and an ongoing duty**, not a one-time flash of genius.

> **What this chapter does *not* repeat.** Chapter 3 already covered **sustaining vs. disruptive** innovation and the **innovator's dilemma** in depth. This chapter assumes that and goes elsewhere: what innovation fundamentally *is*, the *places* it can happen, how it spreads (or fails to), and how to do it on purpose without falling into novelty for its own sake.

## Introduction

Almost everyone agrees that startups are about "innovation." Almost no one can say precisely what that word means — and the vagueness is expensive, because it leads founders to chase the wrong thing.

Here is the trap. The word "innovation" *sounds* like it means "inventing something new and clever." So founders pour themselves into novelty — a new technology, a never-before-seen feature, a clever twist — and assume that *because it's new, it must be valuable.* Then they're bewildered when the world shrugs. The market doesn't care that something is new. It cares whether something is *better*, for a *real problem*, in a way people will actually *adopt.*

This chapter draws the line between *new* and *valuable* sharply, because that line is where most "innovative" startups die. We'll see that the most powerful innovations are often not technological at all; that even genuinely valuable innovations frequently fail to *spread* (and why); and that innovation, far from being a lightning bolt of genius, is mostly a *process* you can learn — and a *duty* you can never stop performing. By the end, you'll understand why the question is never "is this new?" but always "is this new *and* better *and* adoptable?"

## Beginner Explanation

### Invention vs. innovation

Start with the most important distinction in the chapter.

> **Invention** — creating something *new* that did not exist before (a new technology, device, method, or idea).
>
> **Innovation** — creating something new that is also *valuable* and gets *adopted* — that is, it solves a real problem better than the alternatives, and people actually use it.

The difference is everything. An **invention** is merely *new*. An **innovation** is *new + valuable + adopted.* You can invent something brilliant that helps no one — that's an invention but not an innovation. History is full of clever inventions that went nowhere because nobody needed them, they were too expensive, too early, or too hard to switch to.

A simple way to hold it:

```text
   INVENTION   =  NEW
   INNOVATION  =  NEW  +  VALUABLE  +  ADOPTED

   A startup needs an INNOVATION, not just an invention.
   Being new is the easy part. Being adopted is the whole game.
```

The market is indifferent to novelty. It rewards *usefulness that people take up.* This is why "we built something nobody else has" is not, by itself, good news — it might mean you've found a gap, or it might mean nobody wanted the thing badly enough to build it before you.

### "New" is not "valuable"

Let's make the point concrete. For something new to be *valuable*, three things must be true:

1. **It solves a real problem** people actually have (not one you imagine they have).
2. **It solves that problem better** than what they currently use (clearly better, not marginally).
3. **People can and will switch to it** (the benefit outweighs the hassle, cost, and risk of changing).

A new thing that misses *any* of these is an invention, not an innovation. It can be the cleverest technology in the world and still fail all three — solving a problem no one has, only slightly better than the status quo, with too much friction to adopt. We'll see exactly this failure (the Segway) later.

### Innovation isn't only about technology

Here's a liberating idea, especially if you're not a deep-tech inventor: **innovation does not have to be technological.** You can innovate in *many* places:

- **What** you sell (the product itself).
- **How** you make it (the process).
- **How** you deliver it (the channel).
- **How** you charge for it (the pricing/revenue model).
- **Who** you sell it to (the customer / market).
- **How** you bundle or package it.

Many of the most valuable startups in history invented *no new technology at all.* They took existing technology and innovated on the *model* — how it was delivered, priced, or who it served. Uber didn't invent the car, the smartphone, or GPS; it innovated the *model* of summoning a ride. Netflix didn't invent video; it innovated *how video was delivered and paid for.* This matters enormously, because it means **you don't need to be a genius inventor to be a great innovator** — you need to find a better *way*, anywhere in the chain from making to selling.

### The one-sentence version

> **Innovation is not "something new" — it's "something new that is genuinely better at solving a real problem, and that people actually adopt." And it can happen anywhere — in the product, the model, or the process — not just in the technology.**

## Intermediate Explanation

Now let's name the *kinds* of innovation and see why some are more powerful than others.

### The three places innovation happens

Innovation can be sorted by *what* is being made new:

> **Product innovation** — a better or new *product* (a faster chip, a new kind of app, a novel device).
>
> **Business-model innovation** — a new *way of creating, delivering, or capturing value* — a new pricing model, distribution channel, or customer relationship, often using existing products/technology.
>
> **Process innovation** — a better *way of producing or operating* — making the same thing faster, cheaper, or at higher quality (a more efficient factory, a smarter supply chain).

Most people, when they hear "innovation," think only of the first — *product* innovation, especially the technological kind. But the other two are frequently where the biggest startup wins come from.

### Why business-model innovation is often the most powerful

Here is a counterintuitive and important claim: **for startups, innovating on the business model is frequently more powerful — and more defensible — than innovating on the technology.**

Why? Three reasons:

1. **Technology is often copyable; models can be harder to copy.** A clever feature can be cloned by a well-funded incumbent in months. But a fundamentally different *business model* can be much harder for an incumbent to copy, because copying it would damage their existing business (recall the innovator's dilemma from Chapter 3 — the incumbent is *trapped* by their current model).

2. **You don't need to invent anything.** Business-model innovation lets you build on *existing*, proven technology, which is cheaper, faster, and lower-risk than betting on an unproven invention. You combine known pieces in a new way.

3. **It's often what customers actually feel.** Customers rarely care about your underlying technology; they feel the *model* — the price, the convenience, the way they get and pay for the thing. Innovating there is innovating exactly where the customer experiences value.

```text
   TECHNOLOGY INNOVATION              BUSINESS-MODEL INNOVATION
   ─────────────────────              ─────────────────────────
   "We built a better engine."        "We sell/deliver/price it
                                        in a fundamentally better way."

   Often copyable by incumbents.      Often blocked for incumbents
   Needs invention (risky, slow).      (would hurt their existing model).
   Customer may not even notice.       Customer feels it directly.
```

This is why so many giant startups were *not* technological inventors. They were **model** innovators who used existing technology in a new way. (Examples in the case studies below.)

### Incremental vs. radical innovation

A second useful axis is *how big a leap* the innovation is:

> **Incremental innovation** — a small improvement on what already exists (a slightly better feature, a modest efficiency gain). Low-risk, but rarely enough to build a startup on by itself.
>
> **Radical (or breakthrough) innovation** — a large leap that changes what's possible (a fundamentally new capability or model). Higher-risk, but the kind of leap that can create a whole new market.

Incumbents excel at incremental innovation (it's the "sustaining innovation" of Chapter 3). Startups usually need something more *radical* — at least in *one* dimension — because an incremental improvement isn't a strong enough reason for customers to switch *to a risky new company.* Which brings us to the rule that quantifies "strong enough."

### The 10x rule

Why isn't "a bit better" good enough? Because of **inertia and switching costs.** Customers already use something. Switching to you costs them effort, money, and risk. To overcome that friction, you usually can't be 10% better — you often need to be *dramatically* better.

> **The 10x rule** — to displace an established solution, a startup's offering often needs to be roughly *ten times* better (or cheaper, or simpler) on the dimension that matters — not marginally better. A small improvement isn't worth the pain of switching; a giant one is.

> **Switching costs** — the time, money, effort, and risk a customer must spend to move from their current solution to a new one. High switching costs protect incumbents and force challengers to be *much* better to win (detailed in Parts 2 and 11).

The 10x rule is a discipline, not a literal measurement. Its point: ask honestly whether your innovation is *overwhelmingly* better at something people care about, or merely *somewhat* better. Merely-somewhat-better is an invention that won't be adopted — it fails the "people will switch" test. Overwhelmingly-better is what breaks inertia and pulls customers across.

## Advanced Explanation

Now the deepest material: how innovations spread (or stall), why valuable ones still fail, innovation as a process, and the duty to keep doing it.

### Diffusion of innovations: how new things spread

Even a genuinely valuable innovation does not get adopted all at once. It spreads through a population in a predictable pattern, described by Everett Rogers as the **diffusion of innovations.** Different groups of people adopt at different times, and understanding this changes how you launch and grow.

The population splits into five adopter groups:

> - **Innovators (~2.5%)** — risk-loving enthusiasts who try new things first, for their own sake.
> - **Early adopters (~13.5%)** — visionaries who adopt early because they see the benefit and are willing to tolerate rough edges.
> - **Early majority (~34%)** — pragmatists who adopt only once something is *proven* and lower-risk.
> - **Late majority (~34%)** — skeptics who adopt only when it's the established standard.
> - **Laggards (~16%)** — the last to adopt, often only when forced.

```text
   THE TECHNOLOGY ADOPTION CURVE (diffusion of innovations)

   number of
   adopters │              ╱▔▔▔╲
            │            ╱       ╲
            │          ╱           ╲
            │        ╱               ╲
            │  ╱▔╲ ╱                   ╲___
            │ ╱   V                        ╲___
            └─┴────┬───────┬────────┬────────┴──── time
            Innov. │ Early  │ Early  │ Late   │ Laggards
            2.5%   │ Adopt. │ Major. │ Major. │ 16%
                   │ 13.5%  │ 34%    │ 34%    │
                   ▲
                   │
              ┌─────────────┐
              │  THE CHASM  │  ← the deadly gap
              └─────────────┘
```

### The chasm: where most innovations die

Geoffrey Moore added the crucial insight (in *Crossing the Chasm*): there is a **deadly gap** between the **early adopters** and the **early majority** — he called it **the chasm.**

> **The chasm** — the large, dangerous gap between *early adopters* (visionaries who'll tolerate a rough, unproven product) and the *early majority* (pragmatists who demand a proven, complete, low-risk product). Many innovations win over enthusiasts, then fail to cross into the mainstream — and die in the chasm.

Why is the chasm so deadly? Because the two groups want *opposite* things:

- **Early adopters** want something *new and exciting*. They'll forgive missing features and rough edges for the thrill of being first. Pleasing them is relatively easy for a novel product.
- **The early majority** wants something *proven, complete, and safe*. They don't want to be pioneers; they want to adopt what already works for people like them. They demand references, reliability, and a "whole product" — and they're deeply unimpressed by mere novelty.

So a startup can do brilliantly with innovators and early adopters — get great press, enthusiastic users, early traction — and then *stall completely* when it tries to reach the mainstream, because the very novelty that thrilled the early crowd *frightens* the pragmatic majority. Crossing the chasm requires a shift: from selling excitement to selling *proof and completeness*, often by dominating one narrow, specific market segment first (so the majority sees "people exactly like me already use this successfully").

The chasm is one of the most important concepts in this book for understanding why *good* innovations fail. It's not enough to be valuable to enthusiasts. **You must engineer the crossing into the mainstream — and most of the work happens after the early excitement, not during it.**

### Why valuable innovations still fail

The chasm is one reason; let's gather the full set. A genuinely better innovation can still fail to be adopted because of:

- **No real problem.** It solves a problem people don't actually have, or don't have *badly enough* to act (the most common killer).
- **Insufficient improvement.** It's better, but not 10x better — not worth the switching cost (the inertia problem).
- **Switching costs too high.** The hassle, cost, or risk of changing outweighs the benefit, even if the new thing is superior.
- **Bad timing.** Too early (the market, technology, or platform isn't ready — Chapter 2) or too late (someone already locked in the market). *Timing gets its own chapter in Part 2.*
- **Failure to cross the chasm.** It wins enthusiasts but can't reach the pragmatic majority.

Notice that *only the first two are about the innovation itself.* The rest are about *adoption* — which is exactly why "new and better" is necessary but not sufficient. **Innovation is as much about engineering adoption as about creating value.**

### Innovation as a process, not a lightning bolt

A persistent myth is that innovation is a flash of solitary genius — a lone inventor struck by inspiration. In reality, innovation is mostly a *repeatable process*, and treating it as such is far more reliable than waiting for a thunderbolt. The main engines:

- **Recombination.** Most innovations are *new combinations of existing ideas*, not creations from nothing. The smartphone recombined the phone, the computer, the camera, and the internet. Looking for fresh *combinations* of things that already exist is a far more productive habit than trying to invent something from scratch.
- **Problem observation.** Innovations come from noticing real problems closely — watching where people struggle, waste time, or improvise workarounds. The problem comes first; the innovation answers it. (This is the entire subject of Part 2.)
- **Platform-riding.** As Chapter 2 showed, each new platform makes new things possible. Asking "what is *newly possible* now that wasn't before?" is one of the most reliable sources of innovation.
- **Constraint and combination across fields.** Bringing an idea that's normal in one field into another where it's novel (e.g., applying assembly-line ideas to fast food, or subscription pricing to software).

The lesson: **you can innovate on purpose.** Innovation isn't a gift you either have or don't; it's a discipline of observing problems, recombining existing pieces, and exploiting what's newly possible.

### The novelty trap

The flip side of "innovate on purpose" is a warning: do not innovate *for the sake of novelty.* The most seductive mistake for clever founders is building something impressive that solves no real problem — what's often called **"technology in search of a problem."**

> **The novelty trap ("technology in search of a problem")** — building something new and clever first, then hunting for a problem it might solve, instead of starting from a real problem and finding the best solution. Novelty becomes the goal, and usefulness an afterthought.

This is the invention-not-innovation failure in its purest form. It feels like progress (you're building something new and difficult!) but it inverts the right order. Innovation should start from a *problem worth solving* and work toward the best solution — not start from a clever solution and search for a problem to justify it. The Segway (below) is the canonical example.

### Innovation is never finished

A final, sobering point. One innovation is not enough. Recall the wheel of creative destruction from Chapter 3: the same force that lets your startup disrupt an incumbent will, in time, let *some future startup* disrupt *you.* The only defense is to keep innovating — to make innovation a *continuous* practice rather than a one-time event.

Companies that innovate once and then settle into pure execution eventually become the comfortable incumbents that the *next* wave of startups dethrones. Sustained success requires sustained innovation: continuing to improve the product, the model, and the process even after you've won — because the wheel never stops turning. **Innovation is not a milestone you pass; it's a posture you maintain.**

## Why It Matters

This chapter reshapes how you evaluate every idea, including your own:

- **It stops you from confusing novelty with value.** The single most expensive founder error is assuming "new" means "wanted." The invention-vs-innovation distinction forces the right question — *is it better, for a real problem, and adoptable?* — before you invest years in something merely clever.

- **It opens innovation to everyone.** Realizing that innovation lives in the *model and process*, not only the technology, frees you from thinking you must invent a breakthrough. Some of the biggest wins come from a better *way*, not a new *thing*.

- **It warns you about the chasm before you hit it.** Knowing the chasm exists changes how you plan: you don't celebrate early-adopter traction as victory, you treat it as the start of a hard crossing — and you prepare for the shift from selling excitement to selling proof.

- **It makes innovation a practice you can run.** Treating innovation as a process (recombination, problem observation, platform-riding) — and a continuous duty — turns it from a hoped-for accident into something you actively, repeatedly do.

## Real-World Examples

### Case study 1 — Netflix: model innovation, not technology invention

**Situation.** Netflix did not invent video, DVDs, the internet, or streaming technology. Those all existed.

**The innovation.** Netflix repeatedly innovated on the **business model and delivery**: first, DVD-by-mail with no late fees (a pricing/delivery innovation that attacked Blockbuster's model — Chapter 3); then a flat-fee streaming *subscription* (a model innovation built on existing broadband); then original content. At each step, the *technology* was largely off-the-shelf; the *innovation* was in how value was delivered, priced, and consumed.

**The lesson.** A company can become one of the most innovative and valuable of its era *without inventing new technology* — by innovating the **model**. And note *why* it worked against incumbents: its model innovations were ones Blockbuster couldn't copy without destroying its own (late-fee, store-based) business — business-model innovation as a moat (Chapter 3's dilemma in action).

### Case study 2 — The Segway: a brilliant invention that wasn't an innovation

**Situation.** The Segway was a genuine technological marvel — a self-balancing personal transporter, the product of serious engineering. It launched amid enormous hype, with predictions it would reshape cities.

**What went wrong.** It failed all three tests of *value*. It didn't solve a problem people *badly* had (most people could already walk, bike, or drive easily). It wasn't *dramatically* better than the alternatives for most uses. And adopting it carried real friction — high price, awkwardness, unclear where to even use it (roads? sidewalks?). It was a stunning *invention* that was never a mainstream *innovation*.

**The lesson.** This is the invention-vs-innovation distinction in its purest, most famous form. Newness and engineering brilliance are *not enough.* The Segway is the warning label for the **novelty trap**: a remarkable technology that started from "look what we can build" rather than "what problem do people urgently need solved?" — and never found a problem big enough to justify itself.

### Case study 3 — Crossing the chasm (the pattern)

**Situation.** Countless innovative products win passionate early adopters — tech enthusiasts who love being first — generating buzz and early traction, then stall before reaching the mainstream.

**The pattern.** The early-adopter excitement creates a false sense of victory. But the **early majority** (pragmatists) won't adopt on novelty; they want proof, completeness, and references from people like themselves. Products that don't deliberately re-tool for the mainstream — narrowing to dominate one specific segment so the majority sees safe, successful peers — die in the **chasm**, beloved by a few thousand enthusiasts and ignored by everyone else.

**The lesson.** Early traction with enthusiasts is *not* the same as mainstream success, and the two audiences want *opposite* things. The work of *crossing the chasm* — shifting from selling excitement to selling proven, complete value to a focused mainstream segment — is where many genuinely good innovations live or die. Plan the crossing; don't assume momentum carries you across.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Confusing invention with innovation.** Believing that because something is new, it must be valuable. Newness is the easy, cheap part; adoption is the whole challenge.

2. **Falling into the novelty trap.** Building clever technology first, then searching for a problem it might solve, instead of starting from a real problem. "Technology in search of a problem."

3. **Innovating only on technology.** Overlooking that the biggest wins often come from **model or process** innovation, using existing technology in a new way — and assuming you must invent something to innovate.

4. **Being only marginally better.** Offering a small improvement that doesn't overcome switching costs (failing the 10x rule). Customers don't switch for "a bit better."

5. **Mistaking early-adopter love for mainstream success.** Celebrating enthusiast traction and walking blindly into the chasm, unprepared for the pragmatic majority's entirely different demands.

6. **Innovating once and stopping.** Treating innovation as a one-time event, then settling into pure execution — and becoming the comfortable incumbent the next startup disrupts.

## Investor Perspective

Investors are professional skeptics of novelty, precisely because they've seen so many inventions that weren't innovations:

- **They ask "what problem, how much better?" not "what's new?"** A seasoned investor is unimpressed by novelty alone; they want to know the *real problem*, how *dramatically* better the solution is (the 10x test), and whether people will actually *switch.* "It's never been done before" makes them ask whether that's because it's a gap — or because nobody wanted it.

- **They prize business-model innovation and defensibility.** Investors know technology is often copyable, so they look for innovations that are *hard for incumbents to follow* — frequently model innovations the incumbent can't copy without harming its core (Chapter 3). A defensible *model* often excites them more than a clever but copyable feature.

- **They probe the chasm.** For products with early traction, investors check whether that traction is just enthusiasts or a real path to the mainstream. They've watched too many companies win the early crowd and die in the chasm to mistake one for the other.

The takeaway: pitch your innovation as a *dramatically better solution to a real, painful problem, with a plan to reach the mainstream and a reason incumbents can't copy you* — not as "something new." Novelty alone is a yellow flag to a good investor.

## Founder Perspective

For you, this chapter is a set of disciplines that keep your ambition pointed at *value*, not just *newness*:

- **Start from the problem, always.** Resist the novelty trap with a simple rule: never let "look what we can build" come before "what do people urgently need?" The problem comes first; the innovation answers it. (Part 2 is your toolkit for this.)

- **Look for innovation everywhere, not just in tech.** Before assuming you need a technological breakthrough, ask whether a better *model* or *process* — using existing technology — would serve the problem better. Often it will, more cheaply and more defensibly.

- **Demand 10x, not 10%.** Hold your idea to the honest question: is this *overwhelmingly* better at something people care about, enough to overcome the pain of switching? If it's only marginally better, it's an invention that won't be adopted.

- **Plan the chasm crossing from the start.** Don't mistake early enthusiast love for victory. Know that the mainstream wants proof and completeness, not novelty, and prepare — usually by aiming to *dominate one narrow segment* so the pragmatic majority sees safe, successful peers.

- **Make innovation continuous.** Treat it as a posture, not a milestone. The wheel of creative destruction will eventually turn on you too; the only defense is to keep innovating in product, model, and process even after you've won.

## Practical Exercises

1. **Invention or innovation?** List five "new" things you've seen recently (products, features, technologies). For each, decide whether it's an *invention* (just new) or an *innovation* (new + valuable + adopted), and justify it against the three value tests.

2. **Find the model innovation.** Pick three successful companies that did *not* invent new technology. For each, identify *where* they innovated (model? process? delivery? pricing?) and why that was hard for incumbents to copy.

3. **Apply the 10x rule.** Take your own idea (or one you admire). On the dimension that matters most, is it 10% better or genuinely ~10x better than the status quo? If only 10%, what would make it 10x?

4. **Map your chasm.** For an innovative product you know, describe what the *early adopters* love about it and what the *early majority* would demand before adopting. How big is the gap? What would it take to cross?

5. **Spot the novelty trap.** Find (or recall) a product that was clearly "technology in search of a problem." What real problem, if any, did it eventually try to claim? Why didn't it work?

It's deadly because the two groups want opposite things, so winning enthusiasts doesn't carry you into the mainstream — many good innovations die there.
6. Early adopters want something *new and exciting* and will forgive rough edges to be first; the early majority wants something *proven, complete, and safe* and is unimpressed by (even wary of) novelty. The very novelty that thrills the first group frightens the second.
7. The *novelty trap* ("technology in search of a problem") is building something clever first, then hunting for a problem it might solve. You avoid it by always starting from a real, painful problem and working toward the best solution — never letting "look what we can build" precede "what do people urgently need?"
8. Because the same force (creative destruction, Chapter 3) that let your startup disrupt an incumbent will eventually let a future startup disrupt you. Innovating once and settling into pure execution turns you into the next comfortable incumbent to be dethroned; only continuous innovation defends against the turning wheel.
</details>

---

## Summary

The most important distinction in this chapter is between an **invention** (merely *new*) and an **innovation** (*new + valuable + adopted*). The market is indifferent to novelty; it rewards usefulness that people take up. For "new" to be "valuable," it must solve a *real* problem, solve it *clearly better*, and be something people can and will *switch to.* Crucially, innovation isn't only technological — it can happen in the **product, the business model, or the process** — and for startups, **business-model innovation is often the most powerful and defensible**, because it can build on existing technology and is frequently impossible for incumbents to copy without harming their own business.

Even genuinely valuable innovations must *spread*, and they do so through the **diffusion of innovations** — a staged adoption curve from innovators to laggards — with a deadly gap, **the chasm**, between the novelty-loving early adopters and the proof-demanding early majority. Many good innovations win enthusiasts and then die in the chasm because they fail to re-tool from selling excitement to selling proven, complete value. Other valuable innovations fail for related reasons: no real problem, insufficient (non-10x) improvement, high switching costs, or bad timing. The lesson: **innovation is as much about engineering adoption as about creating value.**

Finally, innovation is not a lightning bolt but a **repeatable process** (recombination, problem observation, platform-riding) — and a **continuous duty**, because the wheel of creative destruction eventually turns on you too. The two warnings to carry: avoid the **novelty trap** (start from the problem, never from "look what we can build"), and hold your idea to the **10x rule** (be overwhelmingly better, not marginally better). The question is never "is this new?" but always "is this new *and* better *and* adoptable — and can I get it across the chasm?"

---

## Key Takeaways

- **Invention = new; innovation = new + valuable + adopted.** Startups need the second; newness is the easy part.
- "New" is valuable only if it solves a **real problem**, **clearly better**, in a way people **will switch to**.
- Innovation can be in the **product, business model, or process** — not only technology.
- **Business-model innovation** is often the most powerful and defensible (hard for incumbents to copy, needs no invention, felt directly by customers).
- The **10x rule**: be *dramatically* better, not marginally — switching costs make small improvements not worth adopting.
- Innovations spread via the **adoption curve**; the **chasm** between early adopters and the early majority kills many good ones.
- Early adopters want **novelty**; the early majority wants **proof** — opposite demands, so early traction ≠ mainstream success.
- Avoid the **novelty trap** ("technology in search of a problem"): start from the problem, not the clever solution.
- Innovation is a **repeatable process** (recombination, problem observation, platform-riding) and a **continuous duty**, not a one-time flash.

## Glossary of New Terms

- **Invention** — Creating something new that did not exist before.
- **Innovation** — Something new that is also valuable and adopted: it solves a real problem better than alternatives, and people use it.
- **Product innovation** — A new or better product.
- **Business-model innovation** — A new way of creating, delivering, or capturing value (pricing, distribution, customer relationship), often using existing technology.
- **Process innovation** — A better way of producing or operating (faster, cheaper, higher quality).
- **Incremental innovation** — A small improvement on what exists; low-risk, rarely enough alone.
- **Radical / breakthrough innovation** — A large leap that changes what's possible; higher-risk, can create new markets.
- **The 10x rule** — The discipline that a startup usually must be dramatically (≈10x) better to overcome switching costs and displace an incumbent.
- **Switching costs** — The time, money, effort, and risk a customer spends to move to a new solution (Parts 2, 11).
- **Diffusion of innovations** — The staged pattern by which a new thing spreads through a population.
- **Technology adoption lifecycle** — The five adopter groups: innovators, early adopters, early majority, late majority, laggards.
- **The chasm** — The deadly gap between early adopters (want novelty) and the early majority (want proof); where many innovations die.
- **Crossing the chasm** — Re-tooling from selling excitement to selling proven, complete value to a focused mainstream segment.
- **Novelty trap ("technology in search of a problem")** — Building something clever first, then hunting for a problem it might solve, instead of starting from a real problem.
- **Recombination** — Creating innovation by combining existing ideas or technologies in a new way.
