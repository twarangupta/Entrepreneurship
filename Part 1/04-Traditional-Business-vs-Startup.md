## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

2. Understand the puzzle of why a startup would *deliberately lose money*, and explain it using the idea of investing in growth (the **J-curve**).
3. Explain *why* the two are financed differently — **debt** for traditional businesses, **equity** for startups — from first principles.

7. Decide, for any venture, which type you are actually building — and run it by the right rules.

## Introduction

You now know, from Chapter 1, that a startup grows like a snowball and a traditional business grows like a wall. That's the *behavior*. But two machines can behave differently because they're trying to do completely different things — and that's the real story here.

A traditional business and a startup are not two points on the same scale, where a startup is just a "more ambitious" business. They are answers to two different questions. The traditional business answers: *"How do I build something that reliably makes money and keeps making it?"* The startup answers: *"How do I build something that could become enormous, accepting that it probably won't?"*

Almost every visible difference between them — how they raise money, how they're valued, whether they lose money on purpose, how they end — flows from that difference in the underlying question. This chapter walks through each of those differences and traces it back to its root. By the end, you'll be able to look at any venture and say, with confidence, *"this is being run as a traditional business"* or *"this is being run as a startup"* — and whether that's the right choice.

---

## Beginner Explanation

### Two different goals

Start with what each one is *for*.

A **traditional business** exists to generate **profit** — money left over after costs — as soon and as steadily as possible. The owner wants income. Success means: the business makes more than it spends, reliably, hopefully for many years.

> **Profit** — what's left from revenue after you subtract all the costs of running the business. If you take in $100 and spend $70, your profit is $30. (We'll study profit precisely in Part 5; here we just need the plain idea.) A traditional business lives and dies by profit.

A **startup** exists to build something that could become **very large in the future** — and is willing to spend money *now*, often running at a loss, to get there faster. Success, early on, isn't measured in profit at all. It's measured in growth: more users, more revenue, more evidence that the snowball is rolling.

So the two are optimizing for different things:

```text
   TRADITIONAL BUSINESS                STARTUP
   optimizes for:                      optimizes for:

      PROFIT, NOW                          GROWTH → future value
      (steady income)                      (a shot at something huge)
```

### The strange part: losing money on purpose

Here's something that confuses every beginner. A startup will often *deliberately* spend more than it earns — for years. It loses money on purpose. To a traditional-business owner, this looks insane. Why would anyone run a business that loses money?

The answer is an analogy. Imagine you could plant a tree that, once grown, would produce fruit worth a fortune every year — but it takes five years of watering and care, with no fruit at all, before it matures. During those five years, you're "losing money" (spending on water and care, earning nothing). But you're not wasting money — you're *investing* in something that pays off enormously later.

A startup spends money early to **grow faster** — to acquire customers, build the product, and capture the market before competitors — because if the snowball really is rolling, growing fast now is worth far more than a small profit now. The early losses aren't failure; they're the cost of buying a much bigger future.

This only makes sense, of course, if the big future is real. A traditional business that loses money is usually just *failing*. A startup that loses money *might* be investing wisely — or might also just be failing. Telling the difference is one of the hardest and most important judgments in the whole game, and we'll give you a tool for it later in this chapter ("default alive vs. default dead").

### The one-sentence version

> **A traditional business spends money to make money now. A startup spends money now to *become* something much bigger later — and only the second one is willing to lose money on purpose to get there.**

## Intermediate Explanation

Now we connect the goals to the practical machinery: how each is financed, and how each ends.

### Why they're financed differently: debt vs. equity

Because they're optimizing for different things, the two types raise money in fundamentally different ways. (Chapter 5 goes deep on bootstrapping vs. venture funding; here we only need the *logic* of why the financing differs.)

A traditional business typically uses **debt** — a loan from a bank — or its own profits.

> **Debt financing** — borrowing money that you must pay back, with interest, on a schedule, regardless of how the business performs. The lender doesn't own any of your business; they just want their money back plus interest.

A startup typically uses **equity** — selling a piece of the company to investors.

> **Equity financing** — raising money by selling ownership (shares) in the company. The investor gives you money and, in return, owns part of the business. You never have to "pay it back"; instead, the investor makes money only if the company becomes valuable. (Full treatment in Parts 6–7.)

Why does each type naturally fit one kind of financing? Trace it to the goal:

- A **bank loan** must be repaid on a fixed schedule whether or not the business is thriving. That only works for a business with **steady, predictable cash flow** — exactly what a traditional business has. A startup that loses money for years could never make fixed loan repayments; a bank would (rightly) refuse to lend (recall from Chapter 2 *why* banks can't fund startups).

- **Equity** asks for nothing back on a schedule. The investor is paid only *if* the company succeeds, by owning a slice that becomes valuable. This fits a startup perfectly: it can lose money for years while it searches, owing no repayments, because its investors are betting on the huge future, not collecting interest along the way.

```text
   TRADITIONAL BUSINESS                 STARTUP
   ────────────────────                 ───────
   uses DEBT (or own profit)            uses EQUITY

   "Lend me $X; I'll repay              "Buy a slice of my company;
    $X + interest on schedule,           you get nothing on a schedule,
    because my income is steady."        but a fortune if we win big."

   Risk to lender: low                  Risk to investor: high
   Reward to lender: capped (interest)  Reward to investor: uncapped (the upside)
```

The deep point: **debt suits predictable, bounded businesses; equity suits risky, unbounded ones.** The financing isn't an arbitrary preference — it's the only kind of money whose risk-and-reward shape *matches* the kind of business.

### How they end: the exit

The two types also differ in how the founder eventually gets paid — and even in whether there's a planned ending at all.

A traditional business often has **no ending** by design. The owner runs it for income, year after year, and perhaps passes it to family or sells it eventually to another operator who wants the income stream. The *income itself* is the reward.

A startup is usually built toward a specific event called an **exit** — the moment the founders and investors finally turn their ownership into cash.

> **Exit** — the event where a startup's owners convert their equity (which is just ownership on paper) into real money. The two main kinds are an **acquisition** (a larger company buys the startup) and an **IPO** (the startup sells shares to the public on a stock market). Until an exit, startup equity is "paper wealth" — potentially huge, but not yet spendable. (Full treatment in Part 7.)

Why the difference? Because of the goal again. A traditional business produces a stream of *profit* you can take out as income along the way. A startup, by contrast, usually produces little or no profit for years — its value is locked up *inside* the growing company as equity. The only way to unlock that value is an exit. So the whole startup is, in a sense, aimed at that future moment: build something so valuable that someone buys it (acquisition) or the public does (IPO).

This is why you'll hear startup people talk constantly about "the exit," while a traditional baker rarely thinks about one. The baker is already getting paid, every month. The startup founder is, in effect, deferring almost all their reward to a single uncertain future event.

```text
   TRADITIONAL BUSINESS: reward arrives steadily, along the way
      profit → owner → profit → owner → profit → owner → ...
      (no ending required; income IS the reward)

   STARTUP: reward arrives (if at all) in one big future event
      losses → growth → growth → growth → ──► EXIT 💰
      (value locked in equity until acquisition or IPO)
```

### A quick word on ownership structure

One more practical difference follows from financing. A traditional business is often owned by one person or a family — a simple ownership picture. A startup, because it sells equity to raise money, ends up with **many owners**: founders, investors, and employees (through stock options), all tracked on a document called a **cap table**.

> **Cap table (capitalization table)** — a record of who owns what percentage of a startup. Because startups sell equity repeatedly to fund growth, their ownership becomes a layered structure of founders, multiple rounds of investors, and employees. (Full treatment in Parts 6–7.)

You don't need the mechanics yet. The point for now is simply that a startup's ownership is *shared and complex by design*, because sharing ownership (equity) is how it raised the money to chase its big future. A traditional business, funded by debt and profit, keeps ownership simple and concentrated.

## Advanced Explanation

Now the two deepest differences — how each is *valued*, and how to diagnose a company's true health.

### How each is valued: profit multiples vs. potential

This is one of the most counterintuitive things in business, and getting it backwards causes endless confusion: **a startup losing millions can be worth far more than a profitable traditional business.** How?

Because the two are valued on completely different logic.

A traditional business is valued mainly on its **profits** — specifically, on a multiple of its yearly earnings.

> **Earnings multiple** — a way of valuing a business as some number times its annual profit. If similar businesses sell for "3× earnings" and a business earns $200,000 profit a year, it's worth roughly 3 × $200,000 = $600,000. The logic: a buyer is paying for a predictable future income stream, so the price tracks how much income it produces *now*.

A startup, by contrast, often has *no* profit — sometimes no revenue at all — yet commands a high value, because it's valued on **future potential**, not present profit. Investors are pricing in the *chance* that this company becomes enormous. The valuation reflects the size of the possible future, discounted by how likely it is.

For startups with revenue, a common shortcut is a **revenue multiple** (a number times annual revenue, not profit), precisely because there often isn't any profit to multiply yet — and because fast revenue growth signals a big future.

> **Revenue multiple** — valuing a company as a number times its annual *revenue* (not profit). Used for fast-growing startups that aren't profitable yet. A startup with $2M of revenue growing fast might be valued at "10× revenue" = $20M, even while losing money — because the buyer is paying for the trajectory, not today's bottom line.

```text
   VALUING A TRADITIONAL BUSINESS        VALUING A STARTUP
   ──────────────────────────────        ─────────────────
   based on:  current PROFIT             based on:  FUTURE POTENTIAL
   logic:     "what income does it       logic:     "how big could this
               reliably produce now?"                become, and how likely?"
   tool:      earnings multiple          tool:      revenue multiple / potential
   typical:   ~2–5× yearly profit        typical:   can be 10×+ revenue,
                                                     or huge with no profit at all
```

Why does the market allow this? Because of everything in Chapters 1–3: the startup's *low marginal cost* and *huge potential market* mean that *if* it works, the future profits could dwarf any traditional business. Investors aren't valuing what it earns today; they're buying a lottery ticket on an enormous tomorrow (the asymmetry from Chapter 3). This is also why startup valuations can swing wildly and crash — they rest on *beliefs about the future*, which can change overnight, whereas a profitable business's value rests on *actual current income*, which is far steadier.

The practical warning: **a high startup valuation is not the same as a healthy business.** It's a bet on the future. A profitable traditional business worth "only" $600,000 is, in a real sense, more *certain* than a startup "worth" $20M that's losing money. Both can be the right thing to build — but never confuse a valuation (a belief) with profit (a fact).

### Default alive vs. default dead

Here is the single most useful diagnostic in this chapter — Paul Graham's idea of **"default alive vs. default dead."** It cuts straight through the confusion of startups losing money on purpose.

Ask one question of any company: **if it continues exactly as it is now — same spending, same growth — does it eventually become profitable before it runs out of money, or does it run out first?**

> **Default alive** — on its current trajectory, the company will become profitable before its money runs out. It will survive even if nothing changes (and even if it never raises more money).
>
> **Default dead** — on its current trajectory, the company will run out of money before becoming profitable. It survives *only* if something changes — usually by raising more money or sharply growing.

This reframes the whole "losing money on purpose" puzzle:

- A **traditional business** is almost always **default alive** — it's profitable, so it survives by default. That's the point of it.
- A **startup** is very often **default dead** — it's spending more than it earns, betting that growth (and the next funding round) will save it before the money runs out. Its survival depends on something changing.

Being default dead isn't necessarily bad *for a startup* — it's the normal state of a company investing aggressively in growth. But every startup founder must know, at all times, *which side of this line they're on*, because it changes everything:

- If you're **default dead**, you are on a clock. You must either reach profitability or raise more money *before the cash runs out*. (That deadline is your **runway** — Part 5.) Misjudging this is one of the most common ways startups die: founders who think they have more time than they do, and run out of money mid-search.
- If you're **default alive**, you have a precious freedom: you can choose whether to keep investing for growth or to coast — and you never *have* to raise money on someone else's terms.

```text
                  Does it reach profit before cash runs out?
                              │
              ┌───────────────┴────────────────┐
             YES                               NO
              │                                 │
        DEFAULT ALIVE                     DEFAULT DEAD
        survives on its own.              survives ONLY if something
        (most traditional businesses;     changes: more funding, or
         the safest startups)              fast growth to profitability.
                                           (most fast-burning startups)
                                           → you are on a clock (runway)
```

The reason this diagnostic is so powerful: it doesn't care about valuation, hype, or story. It's a cold question about cash and time. Run it on any company — including your own — and you'll instantly know its *real* situation, stripped of narrative.

### So which are you building?

We can now answer the question that's been hanging over Part 1. To know whether you're building a traditional business or a startup, check which set of answers describes you:

| Question | Traditional business | Startup |
|---|---|---|
| What am I optimizing for? | Profit now | Future scale |
| Will I lose money on purpose to grow? | No | Often, yes |
| How will I fund it? | Debt / own profit | Equity |
| How is it valued? | Multiple of profit | Future potential |
| How does it end / pay me? | Steady income; maybe sold | A future exit |
| Default alive or dead? | Alive | Often dead (on a clock) |

If your honest answers fall in the left column, you are building a traditional business — and you should run it by traditional rules: protect profit, use debt carefully, stay default alive. If they fall in the right column, you are building a startup — and the rest of this book applies. **The fatal error is building one while running it by the other's rules**: a traditional business that recklessly loses money chasing growth it can't fund, or a startup that optimizes for tiny early profits and never builds anything big enough to matter.

---

## Why It Matters

This distinction is not academic — it determines whether you survive:

- **It tells you whether losing money is wisdom or disaster.** The same income statement — "we spent more than we earned" — means *failing* for a traditional business and *investing* for a startup. Knowing which game you're in tells you how to read your own numbers.

- **It tells you what money to raise.** Take a bank loan for a money-losing startup and you'll default on repayments you can't make. Sell equity for a steady traditional business and you'll give away ownership you didn't need to. Matching the financing to the type is fundamental.

- **It tells you what "success" looks like this year.** For a traditional business, success this year is *profit*. For a startup, it's *growth* and *staying funded long enough to find the big thing*. Chase the wrong metric and you'll optimize yourself into the wrong company.

- **It keeps you honest about your real situation.** The default alive/dead test ensures you never confuse a flattering valuation or an exciting story with the cold question of whether you'll run out of cash. Founders who skip this question are the ones blindsided when the money runs out.

## Real-World Examples

### Case study 1 — Amazon: losing money on purpose, for two decades

**Situation.** For much of its early life, Amazon reported little or no profit, often by design. Many traditional-business observers called it irresponsible — a company that "didn't know how to make money."

**The decision.** Founder Jeff Bezos deliberately reinvested nearly everything back into growth — lower prices, new categories, warehouses, infrastructure — rather than showing profit. Amazon chose *future scale* over *present profit*, the textbook startup posture.

**Why it worked.** Because the underlying snowball was real (low marginal cost, vast market), the early "losses" were investments that built an enormous future business. The profits, when Amazon finally chose to show them, dwarfed anything it could have earned by optimizing for profit early.

**The lesson.** Amazon is the clearest possible illustration of the startup's bargain: *deliberately forgo profit now to become something far larger later.* But note the essential precondition — it only worked because the future really was enormous. The same behavior in a business *without* that future would simply be a company losing money, i.e., failing. Losing money on purpose is wisdom only when the J-curve is real.

### Case study 2 — A profitable agency vs. a funded startup: same revenue, different worlds

**Situation.** Consider two companies that both make $1M in revenue this year. Company A is a marketing agency that earns $250,000 in profit and grows ~10% a year. Company B is a software startup that *loses* $2M (it's spending heavily to grow) but is doubling its revenue every year.

**The contrast.** By traditional logic, Company A is obviously "better" — it's profitable; Company B is "losing money." Yet investors might value Company B at many times Company A. Why? Company A is valued on its profit (perhaps ~3× earnings ≈ $750K). Company B is valued on its trajectory — if doubling continues, its future revenue is huge, so a revenue multiple could value it in the tens of millions despite the losses.

**The lesson.** Same revenue, completely different valuation logic, because they're different *types*. Neither is "wrong." The agency is an excellent traditional business (default alive, steady income). The startup is a high-risk bet on scale (likely default dead, on a clock). The danger is judging one by the other's scorecard — calling the agency "unambitious" or the startup "irresponsible." Each is rational *within its own game*.

### Case study 3 — The traditional business that took startup money and died

**Situation (a common pattern).** A founder builds a fundamentally *traditional* business — say, a service business with high marginal cost and linear growth (recall Chapter 1). It's modestly profitable. Excited by startup culture, the founder raises **equity** from investors who expect *startup-scale* growth.

**Why it fails.** Now the founder is obligated to chase hyper-growth the business can't actually produce, because its economics are linear, not a snowball. To show growth, they spend recklessly, turning a healthy default-alive business into a default-dead one — on a clock it can't beat. The mismatch between the *type of business* and the *type of money* destroys a company that would have thrived as a steady, debt-or-profit-funded traditional business.

**The lesson.** This is the single most preventable failure in this chapter: **building one type of business but funding and running it as the other.** Diagnose honestly which type you have, *then* choose financing and goals to match. Startup money is not a prize; it's a tool with a specific shape, fatal to the wrong business.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Confusing a valuation with profit.** Treating a high startup "valuation" as if it were money in the bank. Valuation is a belief about the future; profit is a present fact. Don't spend a belief.

2. **Funding a traditional business with equity (or a startup with debt).** Matching the wrong money to the business — the destroyer in Case study 3. Debt suits steady businesses; equity suits risky, unbounded ones.

3. **Losing money without a real J-curve.** Copying Amazon's "invest in growth, ignore profit" posture when the underlying snowball *isn't* real. Then you're not investing — you're just losing money.

4. **Not knowing whether you're default alive or default dead.** Operating without knowing if you'll run out of cash before reaching profit. This is how founders get blindsided. Always know which side of the line you're on.

5. **Optimizing a startup for early profit.** Squeezing out small profits and starving the growth that was the whole point — building a small traditional business while believing you're building a startup.

6. **Building toward an "exit" that the business type doesn't support.** Expecting an acquisition or IPO from a steady, linear business that no one will pay a startup-scale price for. Exits are for snowballs.

## Investor Perspective

To an investor, the traditional-vs-startup distinction *is* the first filter, for a blunt reason tied to financing: an equity investor makes money **only at an exit**, and only a startup (a snowball aimed at a large future) can produce an exit big enough to matter (Chapter 3's asymmetry, Chapter 2's power law).

So when an investor evaluates you, they're silently checking:

- **Is this a startup or a traditional business in disguise?** A great, profitable, *linear* business is — to an equity investor — uninvestable, because it will never produce the large exit their model requires. They'll pass, even praising the business. (This is why a "no" often means "wrong type for my money," not "bad business.")
- **Is the money-losing justified by a real J-curve?** Investors *want* to see aggressive investment in growth — but only if it's buying a genuinely enormous future. They probe whether the losses are building a snowball or just burning cash.
- **Default alive or default dead — and is the runway long enough?** Investors care intensely about whether you'll reach the next milestone (or profitability) before the cash runs out, because a default-dead company on a short clock is a forced, weak negotiator for its next round.

The takeaway for you: pitch a startup *as* a startup (future scale, a path to a large exit), and never try to raise equity for what is really a traditional business. Match the story to the type, because the investor is matching their money to it too.

## Founder Perspective

For you, this chapter is mostly about an honest act of *self-diagnosis* that must happen before almost any other decision:

- **Decide your type on purpose, not by drift.** Many founders never consciously choose; they default into startup behavior because it's glamorous, or into traditional behavior because it's familiar, without checking which their actual business is. Make it a deliberate decision, and revisit it as you learn (especially after Part 2).

- **Match your money to your type.** Once you know your type, the financing follows: traditional → debt or your own profit, keeping ownership and control; startup → equity, accepting shared ownership and the exit-driven path. Don't take startup money for a traditional business out of excitement, or refuse equity for a real snowball out of fear of dilution.

- **Always know your default-alive/dead status.** This is a number you should be able to state at any moment. If default dead, you're on a clock — know exactly how long it is (your runway, Part 5) and what has to change before it ends. This single discipline prevents a large share of startup deaths.

- **Don't let valuation seduce you.** If you take the startup path, a big valuation will feel like success. It isn't — it's a debt of expectation you now owe the future. Stay focused on the real machine underneath (growth, retention, eventually profit), not the number attached to it.

A money-losing startup can be worth more because its low marginal cost and huge potential market mean its *future* profits could dwarf the traditional business — investors price the trajectory, not today's bottom line.
5. An *exit* is the event where owners convert their equity into real cash — mainly an **acquisition** (a bigger company buys it) or an **IPO** (it sells shares to the public). Startups build toward one because their value is locked inside the growing company as equity (little profit to take out along the way), so an exit is the only way to unlock it. Traditional businesses pay their owners steadily as income, so they don't need one.
6. *Default alive*: on its current path, it reaches profit before its cash runs out (survives on its own). *Default dead*: it runs out of cash before reaching profit (survives only if something changes — more funding or fast growth). (a) A profitable traditional business is default alive. (b) A fast-burning startup is usually default dead, on a clock.
7. Building one type of business but funding and running it as the other — most commonly, raising **equity** for what is really a **traditional** (linear) business, then being forced to chase hyper-growth it can't produce, which turns a healthy default-alive business into a default-dead one.
8. Because a valuation is a *belief about the future* (which can change overnight and rests on the company becoming huge), while profit is a *present fact*. A high valuation on a money-losing company can vanish; it isn't cash and doesn't guarantee survival.
</details>

---

## Summary

A traditional business and a startup aren't two points on one scale — they're answers to two different questions. The traditional business optimizes for **profit now**; the startup optimizes for **future scale**, and is therefore willing to **lose money on purpose** (the **J-curve**: dip into losses to invest in growth, then climb far higher) — but only when the enormous future is real.

Every other difference flows from that. They're **financed** differently because the *shape of the money must match the shape of the business*: **debt** (fixed repayments) suits a steady business; **equity** (paid only via the upside) suits a risky one that loses money for years. They're **valued** differently: a traditional business on a multiple of its **current profit**, a startup on its **future potential** (a revenue multiple, or pure promise) — which is why a money-losing startup can be worth more than a profitable business, and why a valuation (a belief) must never be confused with profit (a fact). They **end** differently: a traditional business pays its owner as steady income, while a startup locks value inside its equity and aims at a future **exit** (acquisition or IPO). And they sit on opposite sides of Paul Graham's **default alive vs. default dead** test — the cold question of whether a company reaches profit before its cash runs out.

The practical heart of the chapter is a diagnosis and a warning. Diagnose, honestly and deliberately, which type you're building — then match your goals, financing, and scorecard to it. The most preventable failure in startup-land is **building one type while running it by the other's rules**, especially raising startup equity for a fundamentally traditional business and then being forced to chase growth it can never produce.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Traditional business and startup optimize for **different things**: profit *now* vs. future *scale*. Every other difference follows from this.
- A startup **loses money on purpose** (the **J-curve**) to invest in growth — wisdom only if the large future is real; otherwise it's just failing.
- **Debt** suits steady businesses (fixed repayments); **equity** suits risky ones (paid only via the upside). Match the money to the machine.
- Traditional businesses are valued on **current profit**; startups on **future potential** — so a loss-making startup can outvalue a profitable business.
- A **valuation is a belief**, not cash. Never confuse it with profit.
- Startups are built toward an **exit** (acquisition/IPO) because their value is locked in equity; traditional businesses pay owners as steady income.
- **Default alive vs. default dead** is the cold test of real health: does it reach profit before the cash runs out? Always know which you are.
- The deadliest, most preventable failure: **building one type but running/funding it as the other** — especially equity for a linear business.

## Glossary of New Terms

- **Profit** — Revenue minus all costs; what a traditional business lives by (detailed in Part 5).
- **J-curve** — The pattern where a startup's finances dip into losses (investing in growth) before rising to far higher profits later.
- **Debt financing** — Borrowing money that must be repaid on a schedule with interest; the funder owns none of the business.
- **Equity financing** — Raising money by selling ownership (shares); no repayment, but the investor owns part of the company and is paid via its success (Parts 6–7).
- **Exit** — The event where owners convert equity into cash, mainly an **acquisition** or **IPO** (Part 7).
- **Acquisition** — When a larger company buys the startup; one main type of exit.
- **IPO (Initial Public Offering)** — When a company first sells shares to the public on a stock market; the other main exit (Part 7).
- **Cap table (capitalization table)** — The record of who owns what percentage of a startup (Parts 6–7).
- **Earnings multiple** — Valuing a business at some number times its annual profit; the usual method for traditional businesses.
- **Revenue multiple** — Valuing a company at some number times its annual revenue; used for fast-growing, not-yet-profitable startups.
- **Valuation** — The estimated worth of a company; for startups, a belief about the future rather than a present fact.
- **Default alive** — On its current path, a company reaches profitability before running out of cash.
- **Default dead** — On its current path, a company runs out of cash before reaching profitability; survives only if something changes.
- **Runway** — How long a company can keep operating before its cash runs out (detailed in Part 5).
