# Chapter 1 — What is a Startup?

> *Part 1 — Startup Foundations*

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

1. Define what a startup actually is — and, just as importantly, what it is *not*.
2. Explain the single most important word that separates a startup from every other kind of business: **growth**.
3. Understand why a startup is better thought of as a *temporary search process* than as a *company*.
4. Distinguish a startup from a small business, a side project, and a large corporation.
5. Explain the core economic reason startups can become enormously valuable very quickly.
6. Recognize the difference between *searching* for a business model and *executing* one.
7. Understand why most startups fail — and why founders attempt them anyway.

---

## Introduction

Imagine two people.

The first person opens a bakery on the corner of her street. She bakes excellent bread, hires two employees, serves her neighborhood, and earns a comfortable living. Ten years from now, she will probably still have one bakery, perhaps two. She is a successful business owner.

The second person writes a small piece of software that lets anyone, anywhere, send money to anyone else with one tap. In its first year it has a few thousand users. Three years later it has fifty million. Ten years from now it may move more money than some banks.

Both people started a business. Both took a risk. Both worked hard. But only the second person started a **startup**.

This distinction is not about technology. It is not about being "cool" or working in an office with beanbags. And it is not about how much money you raise. The bakery owner could raise money too. The difference is something much deeper and much simpler — and once you understand it, almost everything else in this book will make sense.

This chapter is about that one difference. We will build the definition slowly, from the ground up, because nearly every mistake founders make in their first few years comes from misunderstanding what kind of thing they are actually building.

---

## Beginner Explanation

Let's start with the simplest possible version, and add precision as we go.

### A first, rough definition

> **A startup is a new company that is built to grow very fast.**

That's it. That is the heart of it. Hold onto the word **fast**, because it is doing almost all of the work in that sentence.

A new company by itself is not a startup. The bakery is a new company. A new dentist's office, a new law firm, a new plumbing business — all new companies, none of them startups. What makes a startup different is that it is *designed* from the very beginning to grow quickly and to keep growing.

### What does "grow" even mean?

When we say a company "grows," we mean it serves more and more people (or sells more and more) over time. We can measure growth in several ways — number of users, number of customers, or, most commonly, **revenue**.

> **Revenue** is simply the total amount of money a business takes in from selling its product or service, before any costs are subtracted. If you sell 100 coffees at $5 each, your revenue is $500. (We will study revenue in great detail in Part 5.)

A startup wants its revenue (or users) to grow not by a little each year, but by a *lot*. We're not talking about going from $100,000 to $110,000 in a year. We're talking about going from $100,000 to $300,000, or to $1,000,000 — and then doing it again the next year.

### An analogy: a snowball vs. a wall

Think of a small business as **building a brick wall**. You lay one brick, then another, then another. Each brick takes the same amount of effort as the last. After a year of work, you have a wall of a certain size. To get a wall twice as big, you must do twice as much work. Effort and result rise together, in a straight line.

Now think of a startup as **rolling a snowball down a hill**. At the start it is tiny and it takes real effort just to get it moving. But if the conditions are right — the right snow, the right slope — the snowball starts picking up snow on its own. The bigger it gets, the more snow it grabs, and the faster it rolls. Eventually it is enormous, and it is growing *without* you having to push proportionally harder.

A startup is a bet that you can build a snowball, not a wall.

```text
   SMALL BUSINESS (the wall)            STARTUP (the snowball)

   result                               result
     │            ╱                       │              ╱
     │          ╱                         │            ╱
     │        ╱                           │         ╱
     │      ╱                             │      ╱
     │    ╱                               │  ╱
     │  ╱                                 │╱
     └────────────── effort              └────────────── time

   Output grows in step                  Output accelerates over
   with effort (linear).                 time (compounding).
```

### Why "fast" is the whole point

You might reasonably ask: *why does speed matter so much? Isn't a slow, steady, profitable business better and safer?*

For most people, in most cases — yes, it absolutely is. This is important, and we will say it many times in this book: **a startup is not a "better" kind of business than a small business. It is a different kind of business, suited to different goals and different people.** A profitable bakery is a wonderful thing to own.

But the startup is built around a specific idea: that there exist a small number of opportunities in the world that are *so large* and *so suited to fast growth* that the right move is to grow as fast as possible to capture them before anyone else does. When that's true, slow and steady doesn't just leave money on the table — it loses, because a faster competitor takes the whole market.

We'll make this precise soon. For now, the beginner-level takeaway is this:

> **A startup is a new company designed for fast, continuing growth. Growth is not a side effect of a startup — it is the defining feature.**

## Intermediate Explanation

Now let's sharpen the definition and connect it to ideas you'll hear constantly in the startup world.

### Paul Graham's definition

Paul Graham, the co-founder of the startup accelerator **Y Combinator** (which funded companies like Airbnb, Stripe, and Dropbox — we'll discuss accelerators in Part 7), gave the most influential short definition of a startup:

> *"A startup is a company designed to grow fast."*

He added a crucial clarification: being newly founded does not make a company a startup, nor does working in technology, nor taking venture funding. The *only* essential thing is growth. Everything else — technology, funding, the kind of product — follows from the need to grow fast.

Why does technology usually come along for the ride? Because to grow fast, you need to serve a very large market, and you need to be able to serve each new customer cheaply. Software and the internet happen to be extraordinarily good at both of those things. But the technology is a *means*, not the definition.

### Steve Blank's definition: a startup is a *search*

The second great definition comes from Steve Blank, a Silicon Valley founder turned teacher. His version is deeper and, once it clicks, changes how you think:

> *"A startup is a temporary organization in search of a repeatable and scalable business model."*

Let's unpack every word, because this single sentence is one of the most useful ideas in the entire book.

- **Temporary** — A startup is not a permanent state. It is a phase. The goal is to *stop* being a startup — to graduate into a real, stable company once you've figured things out. Most things about a startup (chaos, uncertainty, frequent changes of direction) are features of this temporary search phase, not how you'd want to run a company forever.

- **In search of** — This is the key phrase. A startup does **not yet know** how it is going to make money reliably. It has guesses. It is *searching*. This is the opposite of an established business, which already knows its model and is simply *executing* it. The bakery owner is not searching — she knows exactly how a bakery makes money. A startup is figuring it out as it goes.

- **Repeatable** — Whatever way the startup finds to get and serve customers, it needs to be repeatable. Selling one thing once, through a personal favor from a friend, is not a business. Being able to do it again and again, predictably, is.

- **Scalable** — The model has to be able to grow large without falling apart or becoming proportionally more expensive. (We'll define "scalable" precisely in its own chapter; it is so important it gets its own treatment.)

> **Business model** — the term simply means *how a business makes money*: who the customer is, what you sell them, how you reach them, what you charge, and what it costs you to deliver. "Searching for a business model" means searching for the answers to all of those questions at once.

### Searching vs. executing: the most useful distinction in this book

Hold these two modes side by side, because nearly every piece of practical advice in startup-land depends on which one you're in:

| | **SEARCHING** (startup) | **EXECUTING** (established business) |
|---|---|---|
| Core question | "What works?" | "How do we do more of what works?" |
| Plan | Constantly changing | Stable, followed |
| Customers | Unknown — being discovered | Known and well understood |
| Pricing | Experimental, often wrong | Settled |
| Failure | Expected and useful | A problem to be fixed |
| Right speed | Fast learning | Efficient execution |
| Key skill | Learning quickly | Operating reliably |

A huge number of startup failures come from running the wrong playbook for your phase: trying to *execute* a detailed five-year plan when you're still *searching* and have no idea yet what customers actually want. We'll return to this idea throughout the book, especially in the chapters on the MVP and Product-Market Fit (Part 3).

### How fast is "fast"? Putting numbers on it

"Grow fast" is vague. Let's make it concrete with the rough benchmarks the startup world actually uses.

A common rule of thumb from Y Combinator is that a good early-stage startup grows around **5–7% per week** in its key metric (usually revenue or active users). That sounds tiny. It is not.

Why? Because of **compounding** — growth that builds on itself, like interest in a savings account. A small percentage, repeated many times, becomes astonishing.

```text
GROWTH AT 5% PER WEEK (compounding)

  Week 0:        $1,000
  Week 10:       $1,629   (already up 63%)
  Week 26:       $3,556   (3.5× in half a year)
  Week 52:      $12,640   (12.6× in one year)
  Week 104:    $159,800   (160× in two years)
```

A business growing 5% *per week* multiplies more than twelvefold in a single year. That is the engine startups are trying to start. Most fail to start it. The ones that succeed can become very large very quickly — which is exactly why investors are willing to fund the risky search in the first place (more on that in Part 7).

Contrast this with a healthy small business, which might grow 5–15% *per year*. Both are good outcomes. They are simply different machines built for different purposes.

## Advanced Explanation

Now we go deeper into *why* startups work the way they do — the underlying economics. This is the part that separates founders who merely copy startup behavior from founders who understand it.

### The economic core: high upfront cost, near-zero cost to serve the next customer

Here is the deepest reason software startups can grow the way they do. It comes down to two kinds of cost.

> **Fixed cost** — a cost you pay once (or regularly) regardless of how many customers you have. Example: the cost of *building* a piece of software.
>
> **Marginal cost** — the additional cost of serving *one more* customer. Example: the cost of letting one more person sign up for your already-built software.

For the bakery, the marginal cost of one more loaf of bread is real: more flour, more electricity, more of the baker's time. If she wants to serve ten times as many customers, she needs roughly ten times the ingredients, and probably a bigger oven, more staff, and more space. Her costs scale up almost in proportion to her customers. This is why her growth is linear — a wall, not a snowball.

For a software startup, the picture is radically different. Building the software the first time is expensive — months or years of engineering effort (high fixed cost). But once it's built, the cost of one more person signing up and using it is *almost nothing* — a tiny sliver of server cost (near-zero marginal cost).

```text
            COST TO SERVE ONE MORE CUSTOMER

  Bakery (physical good):     ████████████  high, roughly constant
  Consultancy (human time):   ███████████   high, roughly constant
  Software startup:           ▏             near zero
```

This single fact — **near-zero marginal cost** — is the engine behind most of what makes startups special:

- It is why software companies can have enormous **profit margins** once they're large (we'll define margins in Part 5).
- It is why they can grow to serve millions of customers without their costs exploding.
- It is why they can give the product away free to some users (Part 4, Freemium) and still make money.
- It is, ultimately, why a startup can become worth billions of dollars when it started with two people and a laptop.

Not every startup is pure software, and not every startup has near-zero marginal cost (Uber pays drivers; Airbnb's value depends on physical homes). But every startup is *searching for a model that scales* — one where growing bigger does not require costs to grow just as fast. When marginal cost stays low as you grow, the snowball rolls.

### Scalability as a property of the *business model*, not the product

A subtle but critical point: **scalability is not really about your code. It's about your business model.**

You can write beautifully scalable software and still have an unscalable business — if, for example, every new customer requires three weeks of hand-holding from your team to get value. The *software* scales; the *business* does not, because the cost of acquiring and serving each customer doesn't fall as you grow.

Conversely, a business with some manual parts can still be a real startup if the *overall model* scales. The test is always the same question:

> **As we get bigger, does the cost and effort of getting and serving each additional customer go *down* (or stay flat), or does it rise in proportion to our size?**

If it goes down or stays flat: you have a snowball. If it rises in proportion: you have a wall, no matter how much technology you've used to build it. This question will come back when we study **unit economics** (Part 6) and **SaaS economics** (Part 4).

### Why startups must search before they execute (and why this is so hard)

Because a startup is built around growth, and because the kind of growth it needs only happens when you've found a product a large number of people genuinely want, the startup's first and hardest job is *finding that thing*. This is the search.

The difficulty is that you cannot search efficiently and grow efficiently at the same time. Searching means trying things, being wrong often, throwing work away, and changing direction. Growing means doubling down and scaling up. Do them in the wrong order — scale before you've found what works — and you simply grow a broken thing faster, which burns money and time. This mistake, called **premature scaling**, is one of the most common startup killers, and we'll examine it closely in Part 3 and Part 5.

```text
THE CORRECT ORDER

   SEARCH ──────────────► FIND IT ──────────────► SCALE
   (try, fail, learn,     (Product–Market Fit:    (now grow fast;
    change direction)      people clearly want      pour fuel on
                           what you built)           the fire)

THE FATAL MISTAKE: premature scaling

   SCALE a thing you haven't validated ──► grow a broken
   business faster ──► run out of money ──► die
```

### The base rate: most startups fail

We must be honest about this from the very first chapter, because it shapes everything.

Most startups fail. The commonly cited figure is that roughly **90% of startups ultimately fail**, and even among startups that raise professional venture funding, a large majority never return significant money to investors. The exact numbers vary by study and definition, but the direction is not in doubt: failure is the *normal* outcome, and success is the exception.

This is not a flaw in the system — it is the system working as designed. Startups are attempts to do something that has not reliably been done before. By definition, most attempts at hard, unproven things fail. The startup world copes with this through a particular kind of math, which we'll meet properly in Part 7: investors fund many startups expecting most to fail, because the rare giant winner can return more than all the losses combined. A single Google or Stripe pays for hundreds of failures.

For you as a founder, the practical lesson is twofold:

1. **Failure is common, so failing does not make you a failure.** Most successful founders failed first. Treat each attempt as a search, learn from it, and you're playing the game correctly.
2. **Because the odds are long, you should choose carefully *what* to work on.** Most of the difference between success and failure is decided before you write a line of code — in the choice of problem, market, and timing. That is why Part 2 of this book (Startup Ideas) is so large and so important.

## Why It Matters

You might be tempted to treat the "what is a startup" question as a definition to memorize and move past. Don't. Getting this wrong has concrete, expensive consequences:

- **It tells you whether to take investment at all.** Investors give startups money in exchange for owning a piece of the company (**equity** — we'll cover it in Parts 6 and 7), and they only make their money back if the company grows enormously. If you're building a steady, modest business — a perfectly good goal — taking that kind of investment is a mistake, because you'll be obligated to chase growth you don't actually want. Knowing what a startup is keeps you from raising money you shouldn't.

- **It tells you what "success" means for you this year.** For a startup in its search phase, success is *learning what works*, not maximizing this quarter's profit. Founders who optimize for early profit often starve the search and never find the big thing.

- **It tells you which advice applies to you.** The internet is full of contradictory startup advice. Most contradictions dissolve once you ask: *is this advice for searching or for executing? For a snowball business or a wall business?* The right answer depends entirely on which kind of thing you're building.

- **It protects you from cargo-culting.** Many people copy the *surface* of startups — the funding announcements, the hiring sprees, the offices — without the *substance* (fast growth driven by something people want). Understanding the real definition lets you ignore the theater and focus on the one thing that matters.

## Real-World Examples

Short case studies, used only to illuminate the concept — not as biographies.

### Case study 1 — Google: a snowball with near-zero marginal cost

**Situation.** In the late 1990s, the web was growing explosively and existing search engines returned poor results. Two Stanford students built a better way to rank web pages.

**Why it was a startup.** Search had every property of a true startup opportunity: a gigantic and growing market (everyone using the internet), and software with near-zero marginal cost (serving one more search query cost almost nothing). The model — give search away free, sell advertising against it — was both repeatable and extraordinarily scalable.

**The lesson.** Google is the snowball in its purest form: a product millions wanted, delivered at almost no extra cost per user, in a market expanding under its feet. The fast growth wasn't a lucky accident layered on top of the product; it was baked into the structure of the opportunity. *When evaluating any startup idea, ask first whether the opportunity itself can support a snowball.*

### Case study 2 — The neighborhood restaurant: an excellent business that is not a startup

**Situation.** A chef opens a beloved 40-seat restaurant. It's full every night, profitable, and locally famous.

**Why it is *not* a startup.** There is a hard ceiling on growth. The restaurant can serve only so many people per night. To serve more, the chef must open more locations — but each new location costs roughly as much as the first (rent, staff, equipment) and requires the chef's attention to maintain quality. Costs scale in step with growth. It's a wall, not a snowball.

**The lesson.** This is not a criticism — it's a profitable, admirable business. The point is that *not every good business is a startup*, and trying to force startup-style fast growth (or startup-style funding) onto a fundamentally linear business usually destroys it. Many failed "tech-enabled" versions of local services failed precisely because they wrapped a linear, high-marginal-cost business in startup clothing and then tried to scale it before the economics allowed.

### Case study 3 — Airbnb: searching before scaling

**Situation.** In 2008, three founders couldn't afford rent, so they rented out air mattresses in their apartment during a conference. The idea of paying to stay in a stranger's home struck most early investors as absurd.

**Why it illustrates the search.** Airbnb did not begin by scaling. For a long stretch it grew slowly and was repeatedly rejected by investors. The founders spent that time *searching* — figuring out who the real customers were, why some listings worked and others didn't (professional photos turned out to matter enormously), and how to build trust between strangers. Only after they understood what made it work did the snowball start to roll.

**The lesson.** A true startup opportunity often looks unpromising and grows slowly during the search phase. The job in that phase is not to scale — it's to *learn what makes the thing work*. Scaling comes after, not before. We'll formalize the moment it's safe to scale when we study Product-Market Fit in Part 3.

## Common Mistakes

Mistakes that flow directly from misunderstanding what a startup is:

1. **Confusing "new company" with "startup."** Founders raise venture money and chase hyper-growth for a business that is actually a steady, linear small business. The mismatch creates years of pain.

2. **Believing technology makes it a startup.** Using software doesn't make a business a startup if the underlying model doesn't scale. Always check the marginal cost and the size of the market, not the tech stack.

3. **Trying to execute before searching.** Writing a detailed multi-year plan, hiring a big team, and "launching big" before you've found something people actually want. You can't execute a plan you haven't discovered yet.

4. **Premature scaling.** Pouring money into growth before validating that the product works. This grows a broken business faster and is one of the top causes of startup death.

5. **Optimizing for early profit during the search phase.** Squeezing out small profits early can prevent you from making the bold experiments needed to find the big opportunity. (Profit matters enormously — but it has a time and place, covered in Parts 5 and 6.)

6. **Thinking a startup is "better" than a small business.** It isn't — it's different and far riskier. Choosing the startup path should be a deliberate decision, not a default assumption because startups sound exciting.

## Investor Perspective

When an investor hears about your company, the very first thing they are silently checking is: **is this actually a startup — can it grow fast and become very large?** Because of the math we'll explore in Part 7, most professional startup investors *only* make money on the rare company that becomes huge. A solid business that grows steadily to a few million dollars in revenue is a fine outcome for a founder, but it does not return an investor's fund.

So investors evaluate the *structure of the opportunity* before they evaluate you:

- **Is the market large enough** to support a huge company? (TAM, SAM, SOM — Part 2.)
- **Does the model scale** — does serving each new customer get cheaper or stay cheap? (Marginal cost, unit economics.)
- **Is there evidence of, or a credible path to, fast growth?**

If the honest answer to these is "no, this is a steady linear business," a good investor will (politely) pass — and they're right to, even if your business is excellent. This is one reason a "no" from a venture investor is not a judgment that your business is bad. It often just means your business isn't a *startup* in their sense, which is fine.

## Founder Perspective

From the founder's seat, the most valuable thing this chapter gives you is *permission to choose deliberately.*

You are not obligated to build a startup. The world celebrates venture-backed hyper-growth loudly, which can make founders feel that anything less is failure. It isn't. A profitable business you own entirely, that grows steadily and gives you freedom, can be a far better life outcome than a venture-backed startup that consumes a decade and very probably fails.

But *if* you decide you want to build a startup — if you've found an opportunity with a big market and a model that can snowball, and you're willing to accept long odds in exchange for the chance at something enormous — then commit to what that actually requires:

- Accept that you are **searching**, not executing, at first. Stay flexible. Expect to be wrong.
- Refuse to **scale prematurely.** Find what works first.
- Choose your problem and market with extreme care, because that choice decides most of the outcome before you start.
- Make peace with the **base rate.** Most startups fail; failing well and learning is how successful founders are made.

The rest of this book is, in large part, an attempt to improve your odds inside that game.

**Four kinds of "starting something," compared:**

| | Side project / hobby | Small business | Startup | Large corporation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Primary goal | Fun / learning | Steady income | Fast growth, large scale | Sustain & expand existing scale |
| Phase | Exploring | Executing | **Searching → scaling** | Executing at scale |
| Marginal cost | Varies | Usually high | Designed to be low | Varies |
| Growth shape | None expected | Linear / modest | Compounding | Slowing (already large) |
| Typical funding | Own time | Savings, bank loans | Equity (angels, VC) | Profits, debt, public markets |
| Risk of failure | Low stakes | Moderate | Very high | Low (but slow) |
| Main skill needed | Curiosity | Reliable operations | Rapid learning | Coordination at scale |

**The three classic definitions of a startup, compared:**

| Source | Definition (paraphrased) | What it emphasizes |
|---|---|---|
| Paul Graham | A company designed to grow fast | **Growth** as the single defining trait |
| Steve Blank | A temporary organization searching for a repeatable, scalable model | The **search** and its temporary nature |
| Eric Ries (*The Lean Startup*) | An institution creating something new under extreme uncertainty | **Uncertainty** and disciplined experimentation |

These three are not in conflict — they're three angles on the same object. Growth is the goal; searching is how you find a model that can deliver it; uncertainty is the condition you search under.

## Practical Exercises

1. **The snowball test.** Write down three business ideas (yours or famous ones). For each, answer: *As this business gets ten times bigger, does the cost of serving one more customer go down, stay flat, or rise in proportion?* Classify each as snowball-leaning or wall-leaning. There are no wrong answers — the goal is to train the instinct.

2. **Search vs. execute audit.** Think of any company you know well (your employer, a favorite app, a local shop). Is it currently *searching* or *executing*? What's your evidence? What would have to change for it to switch modes?

3. **Compounding by hand.** Start with $1,000 and apply 6% growth twelve times in a row (a year of weekly growth) using a calculator or spreadsheet. Write down the final number. Then do it again with 1% growth. Notice how large the gap is. This builds intuition for why early growth *rate* matters so much.

4. **The deliberate choice.** In one paragraph, answer honestly: *Do you actually want to build a startup, or a small business, or a side project?* There is no correct answer — but founders who skip this question often build the wrong machine for their real goals. Keep your answer; revisit it after Part 2.

5. **Spot the premature scaling.** Find one news story about a startup that raised a large amount of money and later collapsed. In a few sentences, identify whether it scaled before finding what worked. (We'll meet several such cases — Webvan, Quibi — later in the book.)

## Summary

A startup is a **new company designed for fast, continuing growth** — and growth is its defining feature, not a bonus. The simplest way to feel the difference is the **snowball versus wall** image: a small business grows in a straight line because its costs rise in step with its size (a wall), while a startup aims for compounding growth powered by a model whose costs *don't* rise in proportion as it grows (a snowball).

Two classic definitions deepen this. Paul Graham: *a company designed to grow fast.* Steve Blank: *a temporary organization in search of a repeatable and scalable business model.* The second introduces the single most useful distinction in the book — **searching versus executing.** A startup begins by *searching* for what works (expecting to be wrong, changing direction often) and only later switches to *executing and scaling* once it has found something people genuinely want. Reversing that order — **premature scaling** — is one of the deadliest startup mistakes.

The economic engine underneath all this is **near-zero marginal cost**: building software is expensive once, but serving each additional customer can cost almost nothing, which is what lets the snowball roll. Scalability, though, is ultimately a property of the *business model*, not just the code.

Finally, the honest base rate: **most startups fail.** That's the normal outcome of attempting hard, unproven things, and it's why investors fund many bets expecting a rare giant to pay for the rest. For you, it means failing is part of the game played correctly — and that *choosing the right problem and market* (Part 2) is where most of the outcome is decided.

---

## Key Takeaways

- A startup is **a new company built to grow fast**; growth is the definition.
- **Snowball vs. wall:** startups aim for compounding growth; small businesses grow linearly. Neither is "better" — they're different machines.
- A startup is best understood as a **temporary search** for a repeatable, scalable business model — not as a finished company.
- The crucial distinction is **searching vs. executing.** Search first; scale only after you've found what works.
- **Premature scaling** — growing before validating — is a top cause of startup death.
- **Near-zero marginal cost** is the economic engine that lets startups grow huge without costs exploding.
- **Scalability lives in the business model**, not just the technology.
- **Most startups fail**, and that's expected. Failure isn't disqualifying; choosing the wrong problem is far more costly.
- Deciding to build a startup (versus a small business or side project) should be a **deliberate choice**, not a default.

## Glossary of New Terms

- **Startup** — A new company designed for fast, continuing (compounding) growth.
- **Small business** — A business built for stable, usually modest, income, whose costs tend to rise in step with its size.
- **Revenue** — Total money taken in from sales, before subtracting costs.
- **Growth** — Increase over time in a key measure such as revenue or active users.
- **Compounding** — Growth that builds on previous growth, so a steady percentage produces accelerating absolute gains over time.
- **Business model** — How a business makes money: who the customer is, what's sold, how it's delivered and priced, and what it costs.
- **Searching** — The startup phase of trying things to discover a working business model; expects frequent failure and changes of direction.
- **Executing** — Reliably running a business model you already understand; the mode of established businesses.
- **Scalable** — Able to grow large without costs (and effort) rising in proportion; a property of the business model.
- **Fixed cost** — A cost paid regardless of how many customers you have (e.g., building the software once).
- **Marginal cost** — The cost of serving one additional customer.
- **Premature scaling** — Investing heavily in growth before validating that the product works; a common cause of failure.
- **Equity** — Ownership in a company; investors typically receive equity in exchange for funding (detailed in Parts 6–7).
- **Product–Market Fit (PMF)** — The point at which you've built something a market clearly wants; the signal that the search has succeeded and scaling can begin (detailed in Part 3).
- **Y Combinator** — An influential startup accelerator that funds and advises early-stage startups (detailed in Part 7).
- **TAM / SAM / SOM** — Measures of market size; how big the opportunity is (detailed in Part 2).
