## 📖 About this book

This is a complete reference handbook on building startups, designed to take a reader from **zero business knowledge** to understanding startups well enough to:

- Build a startup and validate ideas
- Build an MVP and reach Product-Market Fit
- Understand SaaS, startup metrics, and startup finance
- Read financial statements and unit economics
- Raise funding and pitch investors
- Hire, lead, and scale a company
- Avoid the common founder mistakes — and think like both a founder *and* an investor

It is written like a published textbook (not a blog, not notes): detailed explanations, analogies, real-world case studies, ASCII diagrams, comparison tables, exercises, quizzes, summaries, and glossaries. The emphasis throughout is **deep understanding over surface-level summaries** — concepts first, stories only where they genuinely strengthen the explanation.

When finished, the book spans **11 parts** and is intended as a reference you can revisit for years.

---

## 👤 Who this is for

- Complete beginners to business
- Software engineers and technical founders
- Solo founders and indie hackers
- Students and first-time entrepreneurs
- SaaS builders

> **Assumption:** the reader has strong technical knowledge but almost no business knowledge. No prior understanding of startups, finance, accounting, venture capital, fundraising, economics, marketing, or sales is assumed. Every new term (MRR, ARR, CAC, LTV, SAFE, dilution, PMF, runway, burn rate, etc.) is explained in plain language before it's used.

## 🧭 How each chapter is structured

Every chapter follows the same consistent anatomy, so you always know where to find what you need:

| Section | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Learning Objectives** | What you'll be able to do after reading |
| **Introduction** | Why the topic matters and what puzzle it solves |
| **Beginner → Intermediate → Advanced** | The same concept taught at three increasing depths |
| **Why It Matters** | The practical consequences for a founder |
| **Real-World Examples** | Short case studies (success *and* failure) |
| **Common Mistakes** | The pitfalls to avoid |
| **Investor & Founder Perspectives** | How each side sees the topic |
| **ASCII Diagrams & Comparison Tables** | Visual, at-a-glance explanations |
| **Practical Exercises & Quiz** | Apply and test your understanding |
| **Summary, Key Takeaways & Glossary** | Consolidate and look up terms |

## 🗺️ Suggested reading paths

You don't have to read straight through. Common routes:

- **Full course (recommended):** Part 1 → 11, in order.
- **"I have an idea, what now?"** Part 1 → Part 2 (Ideas) → Part 3 (MVP & Product).
- **SaaS builder:** Part 1 → Part 4 (SaaS) → Part 5 (Metrics) → Part 8 (Growth).
- **Preparing to raise money:** Part 5 (Metrics) → Part 6 (Finance) → Part 7 (Fundraising).
- **Operator / scaling a team:** Part 9 (Sales) → Part 10 (Building a Company) → Part 11 (Advanced Strategy).

## 🗂️ Repository conventions

- Each chapter is a standalone Markdown file named `NN-Chapter-Title.md` (e.g., `01-What-is-a-Startup.md`).
- Chapters are written to be **read independently** *and* **in sequence** — later chapters forward-reference where a concept is treated in full, and avoid repeating what earlier chapters already covered.
- Links in this README assume chapter files live in the **repository root**. If you move them into a folder (e.g. `/chapters`), update the link paths accordingly.

> **Tip:** If you prefer a `/chapters` subfolder, place all `NN-*.md` files there and change links from `01-What-is-a-Startup.md` to `chapters/01-What-is-a-Startup.md`.
