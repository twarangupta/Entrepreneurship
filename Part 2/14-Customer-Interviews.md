## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

1. Run a customer interview that produces *truth*, not comforting lies.
2. Apply **The Mom Test** — asking questions even your mom couldn't mislead you on.
3. Distinguish **good questions** (about the customer's real past) from **bad questions** (about your idea or their hypothetical future).
4. Recognize why **compliments, opinions, and hypotheticals** are worthless — and what to chase instead.
5. Read **commitment and advancement** as the real signals of demand.
6. Avoid the classic interview traps: pitching, leading, "happy ears," and talking to the wrong people.
7. Synthesize many interviews into patterns that update your hypotheses.

## Introduction

The last chapter said: get out of the building and learn from real customers. This chapter is about the conversation itself — and it contains a hard truth: **most customer interviews are worse than useless, because they teach founders things that aren't true.**

How can a conversation *mislead* you? Because people are kind. When you describe your idea and ask if it's good, the person across from you doesn't want to hurt your feelings. So they say encouraging things — "that sounds useful," "I'd probably use that," "great idea!" — that have *nothing to do* with whether they'd actually use or pay for it. You walk away glowing and validated, having learned a comforting lie. Then you build the thing, and the same people who said "I'd use it!" never do.

This is the central problem the customer interview must solve: **how do you talk to people in a way that gets you the truth instead of polite encouragement?** The answer is a discipline, popularized by Rob Fitzpatrick as **The Mom Test** — a set of rules for asking questions that even someone who loves you (like your mom) couldn't use to mislead you, because they're about *facts in their life*, not opinions about your idea. Master this, and customer interviews become the most powerful tool you have. Botch it, and they become an engine of false confidence. This chapter teaches you to do it right.

## Beginner Explanation

### The problem: people lie to be nice

Start with the uncomfortable foundation. When you ask people about your idea, they tend to lie — not maliciously, but out of politeness. They don't want to discourage you, so they say nice things. The result: **the more you talk about your idea and ask people what they think, the more they'll lie to you** — and the more confident and wrong you'll become.

This means the obvious way to do a customer interview — "Here's my idea, what do you think? Would you use it?" — is exactly the *wrong* way. It practically *invites* the comforting lie. You need a fundamentally different approach.

### The Mom Test

The solution is a simple, powerful idea:

> **The Mom Test** — a way of talking to customers such that even your *mom* (who loves you and wants to encourage you) couldn't lie to you — because you're not asking about your idea at all. You're asking about *facts in their life and past behavior*, which they have no reason or ability to fake.

The insight is this: your mom would lie about whether your idea is good (to be supportive). But your mom *wouldn't* lie about *what she actually did last Tuesday*, or *how much she spends on something now*, or *what tools she uses* — because those are facts, not opinions about you. So you simply *stop asking about your idea* and *start asking about their life.*

The Mom Test rests on three rules:

1. **Talk about *their* life, not *your* idea.**
2. **Ask about *specifics in the past*, not generics or the future.**
3. **Talk less, listen more.**

Let's see each in action.

### Rule 1 — Talk about their life, not your idea

The moment you describe your idea, the conversation is contaminated — now they're reacting to *you*, and politeness takes over. So *don't pitch your idea.* Instead, ask about the *problem* in their life.

- **Wrong (pitching):** "I'm building an app that automates restaurant scheduling. Would that be useful?"
- **Right (their life):** "How do you handle staff scheduling right now? Walk me through it."

The second question gets them talking about their actual life and reveals the truth — how they really do it, how long it takes, what frustrates them — *without* them knowing what answer you're hoping for. You learn far more, and none of it is contaminated by politeness.

### Rule 2 — Ask about the past, not the future

People are terrible at predicting their own future behavior, and "would you" questions invite fantasy. *Hypothetical* questions get *hypothetical* (worthless) answers. *Past* questions get *factual* (valuable) answers.

- **Wrong (hypothetical future):** "Would you use a tool that did X?" → "Sure, sounds great!" (worthless)
- **Right (specific past):** "Tell me about the *last time* you dealt with X. What did you do?" → a real story full of facts.

The past is *real*; the future is *fiction.* "What did you do last time?" cannot be answered with a polite hypothetical — it forces a factual account of actual behavior (revealed preference, Chapter 13).

### Rule 3 — Talk less, listen more

Your job in an interview is to *learn*, which means *they* should be doing most of the talking. Founders, excited about their idea, tend to talk too much — pitching, explaining, leading. Every minute you talk is a minute you're not learning. Ask a good question, then *shut up and listen* — especially to the parts that surprise or worry you.

### The one-sentence version

> **Don't ask people what they think of your idea (they'll lie to be nice). Ask about facts in their life and past behavior — questions even your mom couldn't mislead you on — and then listen.**

## Intermediate Explanation

Let's build a practical toolkit of good and bad questions, and understand *why* each works or fails.

### Bad questions (and why they fail)

These questions feel natural but produce lies or noise:

- **"Do you think this is a good idea?"** — Worthless. Only the *market* can tell you if it's a good idea, not an opinion. And people will say yes to be nice.
- **"Would you buy a product that did X?"** — Worthless. Hypothetical future behavior; everyone says yes; almost no one does.
- **"Would you pay $Y for it?"** — Worthless. People vastly overstate willingness to pay in the abstract.
- **"How much would you pay for this?"** — Worthless. Hypothetical pricing answers don't predict real purchases.
- **"Wouldn't it be great if there were a tool that…?"** — Worst of all (leading + hypothetical). You've told them the answer you want.

Notice the pattern: every bad question asks about *your idea*, the *future*, or *opinions.* These are the three contaminants.

### Good questions (and why they work)

These extract facts about real life and past behavior:

- **"Talk me through the last time you dealt with [problem]."** — Gold. A specific, factual story of real behavior.
- **"What are you using to solve this *now*?"** — Reveals real current solutions and workarounds (Chapter 11).
- **"What's the hardest part about [the problem/process]?"** — Surfaces the real pain in their words.
- **"How much does this problem cost you?"** (in time or money) — Reveals severity and willingness to pay, factually.
- **"What have you already *tried* to solve it?"** — Reveals how painful it is (people only try to fix things that hurt) and what's failed.
- **"What happened the last time it went wrong?"** — Surfaces real consequences and emotion.
- **"Why do you bother doing it that way?"** / **"Walk me through your last week with this."** — Digs into real context.

Notice the pattern: every good question asks about *their* life, the *past*, and *facts.* These are exactly the opposite of the three contaminants.

```text
   BAD QUESTIONS                      GOOD QUESTIONS
   ─────────────                      ──────────────
   about YOUR IDEA      ──────►       about THEIR LIFE
   about the FUTURE     ──────►       about the PAST
   ask for OPINIONS     ──────►       ask for FACTS
        │                                  │
   politeness & fantasy                real, trustworthy evidence
   = lies & noise                      = signal
```

### Compliments and opinions are noise

A crucial mental filter during interviews: **compliments are worthless — even dangerous.** When someone says "I love it!" or "great idea!", that's not data; it's politeness. Worse, compliments *feel* like progress, so they fuel false confidence. The correct internal response to a compliment is not joy but suspicion — *I've probably let the conversation drift toward my idea; let me steer back to facts about their life.*

Similarly, **opinions about the future** ("I would definitely use this") are noise. The only things worth writing down are *facts about the past and present*: what they do, what they use, what it costs them, what they've tried, what went wrong. If your interview notes are full of compliments and "I would" statements, you ran a bad interview.

### What you're actually trying to learn

In each interview, you're hunting for facts that test your hypotheses (Chapter 13):

- **Is the problem real and painful?** (Do they have it? How badly? What do they do about it?)
- **How do they solve it now?** (Current tools, workarounds, costs.)
- **Who has it worst?** (Who's most desperate — your best early customer.)
- **What would a solution need to do?** (Revealed by how they cope and what frustrates them.)
- **Is there willingness to pay?** (What are they *already* spending — time or money?)

You extract these from *stories about their real life*, never from their reactions to your pitch.

## Advanced Explanation

Now the deeper craft: reading true signals of demand, avoiding subtle traps, talking to the right people, and turning conversations into decisions.

### Commitment and advancement: the real signal

Here's the most important advanced concept. Even when you ask good questions, *talk is cheap.* The *strongest* signal that someone actually has the problem and wants it solved is when they give you something of *real value* — what Rob Fitzpatrick calls **commitment and advancement.**

> **Commitment** — when a prospect gives up something they value: their *time* (a meaningful follow-up meeting, not a polite "keep me posted"), their *reputation* (an introduction to their boss or peers), or their *money* (a pre-order, a deposit, a letter of intent).
>
> **Advancement** — the prospect moving to a *next concrete step* in a real buying process, rather than offering a vague "this is interesting."

The principle: **the truth is in what people give, not what they say.** A real compliment is a sale. Real interest is a commitment of time, reputation, or money. If someone says "I love it!" but won't take any next step, won't introduce you to anyone, won't put down a deposit — their love is worthless. If someone is lukewarm in conversation but *pulls out their calendar* for a serious follow-up, or *offers to introduce you to the decision-maker*, or *asks to pre-order* — *that* is real demand, no matter what they said.

```text
   SIGNAL STRENGTH (weakest → strongest)

   "Great idea!"               ▏ (noise — politeness)
   "I'd definitely use it"     ▎ (noise — hypothetical)
   "Email me when it's ready"  ▍ (weak — polite brush-off)
   gives a serious meeting     ████ (commitment: TIME)
   introduces you to the boss  ██████ (commitment: REPUTATION)
   pre-orders / puts down $    ████████ (commitment: MONEY — strongest)
```

This reframes the whole goal of an interview. You're not collecting opinions; you're *probing for real commitment.* And the beautiful thing is that asking for a commitment also *tests* the truth: if the problem is real and painful, people will give you something; if it isn't, they'll deflect with compliments. **Asking for commitment is how you separate real demand from politeness.**

### Subtle traps to avoid

Even disciplined founders fall into these:

- **Pitching too early.** The instant you describe your solution, you contaminate the conversation. Save it for the very end (if at all), *after* you've extracted the facts.
- **Leading the witness.** Phrasing questions that telegraph the answer you want ("Don't you find X frustrating?"). Ask *open* questions ("What's the most frustrating part?") that don't plant the answer.
- **"Happy ears."** Hearing what you *want* to hear — interpreting "maybe" as "yes," remembering the encouraging bits, forgetting the discouraging ones. This is confirmation bias (Chapter 13) live, in the conversation. Counter it by writing down *exact* words and the *bad* news especially.
- **Accepting fluffy answers.** Letting vague generalities ("I usually just deal with it") pass without digging. Always push for the *specific, last instance*: "When was the last time? Walk me through exactly what happened."
- **Selling instead of learning.** Treating the interview as a sales meeting (trying to convince them) instead of a learning session (trying to understand them). In discovery, your job is to learn, not to persuade.

### Talk to the *right* people

Who you interview matters as much as how. Two pitfalls:

- **Don't rely on friends and family.** They love you most and will lie most — they're the *worst* sources of truth (they fail the Mom Test by definition). They're fine for *practice*, but never mistake their encouragement for validation.
- **Talk to real target customers.** You want the actual people who have the problem — ideally those who have it *worst* (the most desperate, your best early adopters, Chapter 8). Their facts are the ones that matter.

Finding these people takes effort (cold outreach, introductions, communities, going where they are), but it's essential — interviewing the wrong people produces confident, useless conclusions.

### How many interviews, and what to do with them

A single interview is an anecdote; *patterns across many* are evidence. There's no magic number, but a useful rule of thumb: keep interviewing until you stop being surprised — until new conversations mostly confirm patterns you've already heard (often a few dozen for a given customer segment). You're listening for the *repeated* pains, the *common* workarounds, the *consistent* costs — the signals that show up again and again across different people.

Then *synthesize.* After each batch, ask: *What did I learn? What surprised me? Which hypotheses were supported, which contradicted?* Update your hypotheses (Chapter 13) accordingly, and decide pivot-or-persevere. The output of interviewing isn't a folder of transcripts — it's a *revised, evidence-based understanding* of your customer and problem, and a clear next test.

### A note on B2B vs. B2C interviews

The Mom Test applies to both, but the texture differs (Chapter 7's customer axis). In **B2B**, you're often interviewing professionals about their work problems — they're easier to find (you know the industry) but you must understand their *organization* (who feels the pain, who decides, who pays — these may be different people). In **B2C**, you're interviewing individuals about personal problems — a larger, more varied pool, where emotion and habit matter more. In both, the rules are the same: their life not your idea, the past not the future, facts not opinions, and probe for commitment.

## Why It Matters

The customer interview is the workhorse of discovery — and doing it right or wrong determines whether discovery *helps* or *harms* you:

- **It's the difference between truth and false confidence.** A bad interview (pitching, leading, collecting compliments) produces validation that isn't real, leading you to build the wrong thing *more* confidently. A good interview (Mom Test, facts, commitment) produces truth you can build on. Same activity, opposite outcomes.

- **It's cheap, fast, and available to anyone.** You don't need money, a product, or permission to run customer interviews — just the discipline to ask the right questions. It's the highest-leverage, lowest-cost learning tool a founder has.

- **It surfaces what you didn't know to ask.** Beyond testing your hypotheses, good interviews reveal *surprises* — problems, customers, or solutions you hadn't imagined — which is often where the real opportunity (and the pivot) is found.

- **Commitment-probing pre-tests demand.** Asking for real commitment (time, reputation, money) doesn't just measure interest — it's an early, honest test of whether anyone will actually *act*, long before you've built anything.

## Real-World Examples

### Case study 1 — The same interview, done two ways

**Situation.** A founder wants to test a meal-planning app idea. Two versions of the interview:

**Version A (bad):** "I'm building an app that plans your weekly meals and auto-generates a grocery list. Wouldn't that be helpful? Would you use it?" → "Oh yeah, that sounds really useful, I'd definitely try it!" The founder leaves thrilled and validated.

**Version B (Mom Test):** "How do you decide what to cook during the week? Walk me through last week." → "Honestly I don't plan — I just figure it out each night, usually order takeout twice." "What did you do the last time you tried to eat healthier?" → "I downloaded an app, used it twice, forgot about it."

**The lesson.** Version A produced a comforting lie (a polite "I'd use it" that means nothing). Version B produced the *truth*: this person doesn't plan, has *already abandoned* a similar app (revealed preference!), and isn't actually in pain about it. The same person, asked correctly, *invalidated* the idea — saving the founder months. *How you ask determines whether you learn the truth or a flattering fiction.*

### Case study 2 — Commitment reveals the real customer

**Situation.** A founder interviews twenty potential customers about a B2B tool. Nineteen are pleasant and encouraging — "interesting," "sounds useful," "send me info." One is more reserved in conversation but, at the end, says: "Actually, can I introduce you to our operations head? And if you build a rough version, we'd pilot it — here's a deposit to reserve a spot."

**The lesson.** The nineteen compliments were *noise*; the one *commitment* (an introduction = reputation, a pilot + deposit = time and money) was the only real signal in the room. The reserved customer — who *gave something of value* — was the real demand, while the enthusiastic nineteen would likely never have bought. *Chase commitment, not compliments; the truth is in what people give, not what they say.*

### Case study 3 — Friends and family fool the founder

**Situation.** A first-time founder excitedly describes their idea to friends and family. Everyone is supportive: "That's brilliant!" "You should definitely build it!" "I'd buy it for sure!" The founder, glowing, takes this as validation and builds.

**What went wrong.** Friends and family are the people *most* motivated to encourage and *least* able to be honest — they fail the Mom Test by definition. Their enthusiasm reflected their love for the founder, not real demand. None of them were even the target customer. The "validation" was pure politeness.

**The lesson.** Never mistake the encouragement of people who love you for evidence. Talk to *real target customers* (ideally those with the problem worst), ask Mom Test questions, and probe for commitment. *The warmth of friends and family is the most seductive false signal there is.*

## Common Mistakes

1. **Pitching instead of learning.** Describing your idea and asking what they think — inviting polite lies and contaminating the conversation. Ask about their life, not your idea.

2. **Asking hypothetical/future questions.** "Would you use/buy this?" gets worthless fantasy answers. Ask about the specific *past*: "What did you do last time?"

3. **Collecting compliments.** Treating "great idea!" as data. Compliments are noise — and dangerous, because they feel like progress.

4. **Leading the witness.** Phrasing questions that telegraph the answer you want, so people simply agree. Ask open, neutral questions.

5. **Happy ears.** Hearing what you want — "maybe" as "yes," remembering the good, forgetting the bad. Record exact words and the discouraging facts especially.

6. **Talking too much.** Filling the conversation with your own words instead of listening. The customer should do most of the talking.

7. **Interviewing the wrong people.** Relying on friends, family, or non-target-customers whose answers don't reflect real demand.

8. **Ignoring commitment.** Measuring interest by words rather than by what people actually *give* (time, reputation, money). Probe for real commitment.

## Investor Perspective

Investors can usually tell, in minutes, whether a founder has done *real* customer interviews — and it strongly shapes their confidence:

- **They listen for facts, not founder opinions.** A founder who can recount *specific* things real customers said and did — the exact pain, the current workaround, the cost, the surprising detail — signals genuine, disciplined discovery. A founder who only offers their own confident assessment ("customers will love this") signals they've been collecting compliments, not facts.

- **They prize evidence of commitment.** The strongest possible early validation a founder can show is *commitment*: customers who've given time, made introductions, signed letters of intent, or pre-ordered. Investors weight a single pre-order or pilot far more heavily than a hundred enthusiastic "I'd use it"s, for exactly the reasons in this chapter.

- **They're wary of friends-and-family validation.** Seasoned investors discount enthusiasm from people close to the founder, knowing it reflects support rather than demand. They want to hear about *real target customers* who had no reason to be nice.

The takeaway: do real Mom Test interviews, record *facts*, and — above all — gather *commitment.* When you fundraise (Part 7), specific customer facts and evidence of commitment are among the most persuasive things you can bring.

## Founder Perspective

For you, the customer interview is a learnable craft that fights your own enthusiasm:

- **Stop pitching; start asking about their life.** Your instinct will scream to describe your brilliant idea. Resist it. The moment you pitch, you contaminate the well. Ask how they *currently* handle the problem and *what they did last time* — and learn the truth.

- **Hunt for facts about the past; discard opinions about the future.** Treat every "I would…" and "great idea!" as noise. Treat every "last time I…", "I currently use…", "it cost me…" as gold. If your notes are full of the former, you ran a bad interview.

- **Probe for commitment — it's the real test.** Don't end an interview satisfied with warm words. Ask for something real: a serious follow-up, an introduction, a pre-order, a deposit. What they *give* tells you the truth that what they *say* hides. Asking is uncomfortable; do it anyway — it's the most honest signal you can get.

- **Talk to real customers, not your fan club.** Put in the effort to reach actual target customers — especially those with the problem worst. Use friends and family to *practice* the technique, never to *validate* the idea.

- **Listen for surprises and follow them.** The most valuable interviews often reveal something you didn't expect — a different pain, a different customer, a different solution. That surprise is frequently where the real opportunity (and your pivot, Chapter 13) lives. Stay alert to it, and let it redirect you.

## Practical Exercises

1. **Rewrite five bad questions.** Take five "would you…/do you think…" questions about your idea and rewrite each as a Mom Test question about the customer's real past and present.

2. **Run three interviews.** Find three *real target customers* (not friends/family). Ask only about their life and past behavior — no pitching. Record their *exact words* and the *facts* (what they do, use, spend, have tried).

3. **Signal audit.** Review your interview notes. Highlight compliments and hypotheticals in one color (noise) and facts about past behavior in another (signal). Which dominates? What does that say about your technique?

4. **Ask for commitment.** In your next interview, end by asking for something real (a follow-up meeting, an introduction, a pre-order/deposit). Note what they give — or don't. What does it reveal that their words didn't?

5. **Find who has it worst.** Across your interviews, identify the person in the most pain about the problem. What makes them different? They may be your ideal early customer.

6. **Synthesize.** After several interviews, write down: the repeated pains, the common current solutions, the typical cost, and what surprised you. Which of your hypotheses were supported, and which need a pivot?

## Summary

The customer interview is the workhorse of discovery, but most interviews *mislead* founders because people are polite — ask them about your idea and they'll say encouraging things that have nothing to do with real demand, leaving you confidently wrong. The solution is **The Mom Test**: ask questions even your mom couldn't use to mislead you, because they're about *facts in their life*, not opinions about your idea. Its three rules — **talk about their life, not your idea; ask about specifics in the past, not the future; talk less and listen more** — systematically strip out the three contaminants (your idea, the future, opinions) that invite lies.

In practice this means replacing **bad questions** ("would you use this?", "do you think it's a good idea?") with **good questions** ("how do you handle this now?", "tell me about the last time you faced this", "what have you already tried?"). **Compliments, opinions, and hypotheticals are noise** — even dangerous, because they feel like progress; only *facts about real past behavior* are signal. The strongest signal of all is **commitment and advancement** — when a prospect gives up something they value (time, reputation, or money) and takes a real next step. The truth is in what people *give*, not what they *say*, so probing for commitment is how you separate genuine demand from politeness.

The classic traps — pitching, leading, "happy ears," and relying on friends and family — all reduce to letting your own enthusiasm contaminate the search for truth. Talk to *real target customers* (especially those with the problem worst), run enough interviews to see patterns, listen hard for surprises, and synthesize what you learn into updated hypotheses and a pivot-or-persevere decision. Done right, the customer interview is the cheapest, most powerful truth-finding tool a founder has; done wrong, it's an engine of false confidence.

## Glossary of New Terms

- **Customer interview** — A conversation with a (potential) customer to learn the truth about their problems and behavior; the main tool of customer discovery.
- **The Mom Test** — A method of asking questions even someone who loves you couldn't use to mislead you, by focusing on facts in their life rather than opinions about your idea.
- **Leading question** — A question phrased to telegraph the answer you want, biasing the response.
- **Hypothetical question** — A question about imagined future behavior ("would you…?"), which produces worthless fantasy answers.
- **Commitment** — When a prospect gives up something they value (time, reputation, or money), the strongest signal of real demand.
- **Advancement** — A prospect moving to a real next step in a buying process.
- **Happy ears** — Hearing what you want to hear; confirmation bias operating live in a conversation.
- **Signal vs. noise** — Distinguishing trustworthy evidence (facts about past behavior, commitment) from worthless input (compliments, hypotheticals).

---

> **Looking ahead:** Interviews give you deep, qualitative truth about individual customers. But you also need the *bigger picture* — how large the market is, what trends shape it, and who else is in it. Chapter 15, *Market Research*, broadens the lens from talking to individuals to understanding the whole market: how to research a market's size, structure, customers, and dynamics — combining the qualitative truth of interviews with quantitative understanding of the landscape you're entering.
