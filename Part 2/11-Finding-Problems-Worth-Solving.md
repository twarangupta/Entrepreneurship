# Chapter 11 — Finding Problems Worth Solving

> *Part 2 — Startup Ideas*

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

1. Explain why great startups begin with **problems**, not ideas or solutions — and why this order matters so much.
2. Distinguish a **real, painful, valuable problem** from an imaginary or trivial one.
3. Apply the test of a problem worth solving: is it **real, painful, frequent, expensive, and widespread**?
4. Understand the **painkiller vs. vitamin** distinction and the "**hair-on-fire**" problem.
5. Know *where* the best problems hide and how to actively hunt for them.
6. Dig from a surface **symptom** to the **root problem** (the "5 Whys").
7. Recognize **tarpit ideas** — problems that look appealing but have killed many startups.

---

## Introduction

Part 1 told you what game you're playing. Part 2 is where you start playing it — and the first move, the one that decides more than almost anything that follows, is choosing *what problem to solve.*

Most first-time founders get this backwards. They start with a *solution* — "I want to build an app that does X" — and only afterward go looking for someone who needs it. This feels natural (the solution is the exciting part) but it's the single most common way founders waste years building things nobody wants. We saw the deep version of this error in Chapter 8: the **novelty trap**, "technology in search of a problem." This chapter is the antidote, and it begins with one principle that we'll build the entire part on:

> **Great startups start with a problem, not a solution.**

The reason is simple but profound: *value comes from solving problems.* A solution that doesn't solve a real problem creates no value, no matter how clever it is (Chapter 8). The problem is the source of the value; the solution is just the delivery mechanism. So the founder's first job isn't to be inventive — it's to be a careful, almost obsessive *hunter of real problems.* This chapter teaches you to find problems that are actually worth solving, to tell them apart from the imaginary and trivial ones that trap so many founders, and to dig beneath surface symptoms to the real pain underneath.

## Beginner Explanation

### Why problems come before solutions

Imagine two founders.

**Founder A** thinks: *"I want to build a beautiful app with a clever feature I just thought of."* She builds it, launches it, and discovers that while it's clever, nobody actually needs it. She's spent a year on a solution to a problem that didn't exist.

**Founder B** thinks: *"I keep noticing that small restaurant owners waste hours every week on their schedules, hate it, and complain about it constantly."* She studies that problem deeply, then builds the simplest thing that solves it. People want it immediately, because she started from a pain they already feel.

Founder B is following the right order. She started with a *problem people already have* and worked toward a solution. Founder A started with a *solution* and went hunting for a problem to justify it — and lost.

Here's why problems-first is so powerful, in plain terms:

- **You can change your solution; the problem is your anchor.** Startups almost always change their solution many times (we'll call this *iterating* and *pivoting* later). But if you've anchored on a *real problem*, those changes are just better and better attempts at the same valuable target. If you anchored on a *solution*, changing it means starting over.
- **A real problem guarantees there's value to capture.** If people genuinely struggle with something, solving it *will* create value. The only question left is whether your particular solution is good enough — a much better position than hoping a problem exists for your solution.
- **Problems tell you what to build; solutions you invent tell you nothing.** When you understand a problem deeply, the right solution often becomes obvious. When you start from a solution, you're guessing about the problem.

```text
   THE RIGHT ORDER                  THE TRAP (Ch. 8's novelty trap)

   PROBLEM ──► understand it ──►     SOLUTION ──► go hunt for a
   build the best solution           problem to justify it
        │                                 │
        ▼                                 ▼
   value (problem was real)          usually: no real problem → failure
```

### Not all problems are worth solving

Now the crucial refinement: *most problems are not worth building a startup around.* A problem worth solving has specific qualities. The biggest beginner mistake after "solution-first" is picking a problem that's real but too small, too rare, or too mild to support a business.

A problem worth solving is:

1. **Real** — people actually have it (not one you imagine they have).
2. **Painful** — it genuinely hurts; people are bothered enough to want it gone.
3. **Frequent** — it happens often, not once in a lifetime.
4. **Expensive** — it costs people real time or money (so they'll pay to solve it).
5. **Widespread** — enough people have it to form a market (Chapter 16: TAM/SAM/SOM).

A problem that misses these — real but mild, or painful but rare, or frequent but cheap to ignore — usually can't support a startup. The art is finding problems that hit *all five.*

### The one-sentence version

> **Start with a real, painful, frequent, expensive, and widespread problem — then build the solution. The problem is the source of value; the solution is just how you deliver it.**

## Intermediate Explanation

Let's sharpen the tools for telling a great problem from a poor one.

### Painkillers vs. vitamins

The most useful single distinction in problem-hunting:

> **Painkiller** — a product that solves an *urgent, painful* problem people are actively trying to fix. They seek it out, adopt it eagerly, and pay readily, because the pain is real and present.
>
> **Vitamin** — a product that's *nice to have* but solves no urgent pain. It might make life marginally better, but no one is desperately looking for it, so it's hard to get people to adopt or pay for it.

The best startups are **painkillers**, not vitamins. A painkiller sells itself, because the customer already *wants relief* — your job is just to provide it. A vitamin must be *pushed* on people who weren't looking for it, which is brutally hard.

This connects directly to Chapter 8's 10x rule. A vitamin is, almost by definition, only marginally better than going without — so it never overcomes switching costs and inertia. A painkiller addresses pain so real that switching to relief is obviously worth it. **When evaluating a problem, ask bluntly: is the pain bad enough that people are actively seeking relief? Or is this just a "nice idea" no one is losing sleep over?**

### The "hair-on-fire" problem

A vivid way to capture the painkiller idea: the **hair-on-fire problem.**

> **Hair-on-fire problem** — a problem so urgent and painful that the person experiencing it would grab *any* reasonable solution immediately — like someone whose hair is on fire reaching for a bucket of water without hesitation.

If someone's hair is on fire, you don't need to convince them they have a problem, or persuade them to act, or polish your product to perfection. They want the water *now.* That's the kind of problem you want — one where the customer is already desperate for a solution, so you're meeting demand rather than manufacturing it. The opposite — a "nice to have, maybe someday" problem — means you'll spend all your energy just convincing people they have a problem at all, which is exhausting and usually fatal.

```text
   HAIR-ON-FIRE PROBLEM              "NICE TO HAVE" PROBLEM
   ─────────────────────            ──────────────────────
   "I need a solution NOW."         "Hmm, that might be nice someday."
   Seeks you out.                   You must chase and convince them.
   Pays readily.                    Resists paying.
   = PAINKILLER (sells itself)      = VITAMIN (must be pushed)
```

### Severity × frequency: the problem matrix

Two of the five qualities — *painful* (severity) and *frequent* (frequency) — interact in a useful way. Plot any problem on these two axes:

```text
                    HOW PAINFUL? (severity)
                   mild              severe
                ┌──────────────┬──────────────┐
       frequent │  annoying    │  ★ BEST ★    │
   HOW          │  (often, but │  (often AND  │
   OFTEN?       │   minor)     │   painful)   │
                ├──────────────┼──────────────┤
       rare     │  ignore it   │  big but     │
                │  (minor &    │  rare        │
                │   rare)      │  (one-time)  │
                └──────────────┴──────────────┘
```

- **Frequent + severe (top-right): the best startup problems.** Painful *and* recurring — the customer feels the pain over and over, so they urgently want a lasting solution and will pay for ongoing relief. This is the sweet spot.
- **Frequent + mild:** an annoyance. People notice it but it's not bad enough to act — usually a vitamin.
- **Rare + severe:** genuinely painful but happens once in a long while (e.g., a problem you face only when moving house). Hard to build a recurring business on, though sometimes viable.
- **Rare + mild:** ignore it entirely.

Aim for the top-right. The best problems are ones people hit *frequently* and find *genuinely painful* — because that combination creates urgent, repeated, paying demand.

### Would they pay? The willingness-to-pay test

A practical shortcut that cuts through self-deception: **are people already spending time or money trying to solve this?**

> **Willingness to pay** — whether (and how much) people would actually pay to solve a problem. The strongest signal isn't what people *say*; it's what they *already do* — money or time they're currently spending on the problem.

If people are already paying for clumsy alternatives, hiring someone to handle it, or spending hours on manual workarounds, the problem is real and valuable — they've *proven* it with their behavior. If no one is spending anything to solve it, be very suspicious: either the problem isn't painful enough to act on (a vitamin), or it isn't real. (We'll test willingness to pay rigorously in Chapters 13–14 and 21; for now, it's a fast gut-check.)

## Advanced Explanation

Now the deeper craft: where problems hide, how to dig beneath symptoms, and which appealing problems are traps.

### Where the best problems hide

Great problems don't usually announce themselves. Here's where experienced founders hunt:

**1. Your own life and work.** The classic "scratch your own itch." Problems *you* personally experience are ones you understand deeply, can feel the pain of, and can validate against your own behavior. Many great startups (Chapter 5's examples among them) began as a founder solving their own frustration. The advantage: you have built-in **founder-market fit** (Chapter 12) and instant access to at least one real, frustrated user — you.

**2. Industries you know deeply.** If you've worked in a field, you've seen its inefficiencies, its hated tools, its manual workarounds — problems invisible to outsiders. Deep domain knowledge surfaces problems others can't see (again, founder-market fit).

**3. Workarounds people have built.** When people invent clumsy manual workarounds — elaborate spreadsheets, chains of tools duct-taped together, hiring someone to do something by hand — that's a *flashing signal* of an unmet need. The workaround *is* the problem, made visible. People only build workarounds for problems painful enough to be worth the effort.

> **Workaround** — a makeshift, often clumsy way people cope with a problem because no good solution exists. Workarounds are one of the strongest signals of a real, painful, unsolved problem.

**4. Things people complain about.** Repeated, emphatic complaints — in forums, reviews, conversations, support tickets — map real pain. Listen for "I hate that…", "Why is it so hard to…", "I wish there was…". These phrases are problem-detectors.

**5. What's newly possible (the platform lens).** From Chapter 2: each new platform makes new problems *solvable* that weren't before. A problem people simply lived with because it was unsolvable can suddenly become a startup opportunity when a new platform (mobile, cloud, AI) makes a solution possible. Ask: *what problems were unsolvable before, that just became solvable?*

**6. Living at the frontier.** Founders at the cutting edge of a field — the latest technology, a fast-changing industry — see emerging problems before anyone else, because they hit them first. Being "in the future" (using the newest tools, working in the newest area) is a problem-discovery advantage.

```text
   WHERE PROBLEMS HIDE

   Your own frustration ───┐
   Industries you know ────┤
   Clumsy workarounds ─────┼──► REAL, PAINFUL, UNSOLVED PROBLEMS
   Loud complaints ────────┤
   What's newly possible ──┤
   The frontier ───────────┘
```

### From symptom to root problem: the 5 Whys

A subtle but vital skill: distinguishing a **symptom** from the **root problem.** Founders often build solutions to symptoms, which is like treating a fever without curing the infection. The real, valuable problem usually lies *beneath* the surface complaint.

A simple technique, borrowed from manufacturing, is the **"5 Whys"** — keep asking "why?" until you reach the root.

> **The 5 Whys** — repeatedly asking "why?" (roughly five times) to drill from a surface symptom down to the underlying root cause — which is usually the real problem worth solving.

Example:
- *"Restaurant owners hate making the weekly staff schedule."* — **Why?**
- Because it takes hours every week. — **Why so long?**
- Because they juggle everyone's availability, time-off requests, and shift rules by hand. — **Why by hand?**
- Because their current tools (paper, generic spreadsheets) don't understand any of those constraints. — **Why does that matter?**
- Because a mistake means an unstaffed shift or an angry employee, which costs money and stress.

The *symptom* was "scheduling is annoying." The *root problem* is "there's no tool that handles the real constraints of restaurant scheduling, so a costly, error-prone manual process is the only option." The root problem is far more specific, more clearly painful (it costs money and stress), and points directly at what to build. **Always dig past the symptom to the root — that's where the valuable, buildable problem lives.**

### Tarpit ideas: appealing problems that trap founders

A warning that saves years. Some problems are *seductive* — they sound appealing, lots of people seem to have them, and new founders are drawn to them again and again — yet they've quietly killed enormous numbers of startups. These are called **tarpit ideas.**

> **Tarpit idea** — a problem/idea that's superficially attractive and attempted by many founders, but has hidden, fatal difficulties that have caused most attempts to fail. Like a tar pit, it lures you in and traps you.

Classic tarpits tend to be common consumer problems that "everyone" has and that countless founders try to solve: apps to coordinate plans with friends, generic "marketplace for local services," tools to help people make decisions in groups, and similar. They're tarpits *because* they're appealing — so many people attempt them that the space is littered with corpses, usually for non-obvious reasons (the problem isn't painful *enough* to change behavior, the market is fragmented, distribution is impossible, or people say they want it but won't actually use it).

The lesson isn't "never touch a popular problem" — it's: **if a problem seems obvious and lots of people have clearly tried it, ask hard why they all failed before assuming you'll be the exception.** Often the answer reveals a hidden difficulty that should change your mind, or at least your approach. The most dangerous problems are the ones that feel easy.

### Real, but not real *enough*

A final advanced nuance. Many problems are *technically* real but not *painful enough to act on.* People will *agree* the problem exists ("yes, that's annoying") but won't actually change their behavior or pay to fix it. This is the gap between a problem people *acknowledge* and a problem people are *motivated to solve* — and it's where many "validated" ideas die. The acknowledgment is cheap; the motivation is what matters. This is exactly why the *willingness-to-pay* and *behavior* tests (what people already *do* about the problem) matter more than what they *say* — a theme we'll make rigorous in customer discovery (Chapter 13) and validation (Chapter 21).

## Why It Matters

Choosing the problem is the highest-leverage decision a founder makes, and getting it right (or wrong) echoes through everything:

- **It determines whether there's any value to capture.** A real, painful problem guarantees value exists to be created; an imaginary or trivial one guarantees there isn't, no matter how good your execution. You can't out-execute a bad problem choice.

- **It anchors you through inevitable change.** You'll change your solution many times. Anchoring on a *real problem* means every change is progress toward the same valuable target; anchoring on a solution means every change is a restart.

- **It decides how hard everything downstream will be.** A painkiller problem makes selling, marketing, and growth dramatically easier — customers come to you. A vitamin problem makes all of those a grinding uphill battle. Much of a startup's apparent "execution difficulty" is really just the difficulty of the problem it chose.

- **It protects you from the most expensive mistakes.** Solution-first thinking, vitamins, and tarpits each cost founders *years.* The disciplines in this chapter — problems first, the five tests, the painkiller bar, symptom-to-root digging — are cheap insurance against the costliest errors there are.

## Real-World Examples

### Case study 1 — Scratching your own itch: a problem the founders lived

**Situation (a recurring pattern).** Many enduring startups began with founders solving a problem they *personally* suffered — a tool they desperately wished existed for their own work or life, and couldn't find.

**Why it works.** Founders solving their own problem start with three huge advantages: they *know* the problem is real (they live it), they understand it deeply (built-in founder-market fit, Chapter 12), and they have at least one brutally honest user — themselves — to validate against. They can feel immediately whether a solution actually relieves the pain.

**The lesson.** "Scratch your own itch" is popular advice for good reason: it nearly guarantees you're solving a *real* problem (yours) and gives you deep insight into it. The caution: confirm that *enough other people* share your itch (the "widespread" test) — a problem only *you* have is a hobby, not a startup.

### Case study 2 — The workaround as a signal

**Situation.** Across countless industries, people cope with unsolved problems by building elaborate manual workarounds — giant fragile spreadsheets, chains of mismatched tools, or hiring someone just to do a tedious task by hand.

**Why it matters.** Each workaround is a person *telling you, through their behavior,* that they have a problem painful enough to spend real effort on — but that no good solution exists. Many successful startups were built simply by noticing a widespread workaround and replacing it with a real product. The workaround proves the problem is real *and* that people are already willing to spend time/money on it (willingness to pay, demonstrated).

**The lesson.** Hunt for workarounds. When you see people going to absurd lengths to cope with something, you've likely found a real, painful, validated problem — with the bonus that the workaround shows you exactly what a good solution must do.

### Case study 3 — A tarpit: the "coordinate with friends" app

**Situation.** A perennially popular idea among new founders is an app to help groups of friends coordinate plans (where to eat, when to meet, what to do). It *sounds* obviously useful — everyone has experienced the pain of group coordination.

**Why it traps.** Despite hundreds of attempts, almost all fail. The hidden difficulties: the pain, while real, is mild and infrequent enough that people don't change their habit (they just use group chat); getting an entire friend group to adopt a *new* app simultaneously is brutally hard (a coordination/distribution problem); and people *say* they want it but won't actually switch. It fails the painkiller test (it's a vitamin), the frequency-severity test (annoying but mild), and the adoption test all at once.

**The lesson.** The most seductive problems — obvious, universal, "why doesn't this exist?" — are often tarpits *precisely because* they're seductive: everyone tries them, and there's usually a non-obvious reason they all fail. Before charging at an obvious problem, ask *why the graveyard is so full* — the answer often reveals the trap.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Solution-first thinking.** Falling in love with a solution and hunting for a problem to justify it (Chapter 8's novelty trap). Start with the problem, always.

2. **Building a vitamin.** Solving a "nice to have" problem no one is desperate about, then struggling endlessly to convince people they need it. Aim for painkillers.

3. **Picking a real but trivial or rare problem.** Solving something real that's too mild, too infrequent, or too cheap-to-ignore to support a business. Demand all five qualities (real, painful, frequent, expensive, widespread).

4. **Trusting words over behavior.** Believing people who *say* a problem matters, without checking what they actually *do* about it (or pay for it). Acknowledgment is cheap; behavior is truth.

5. **Solving the symptom, not the root.** Building for the surface complaint instead of digging (5 Whys) to the real, more specific, more valuable underlying problem.

6. **Charging into a tarpit.** Attacking a seductive, obvious, much-attempted problem without asking why so many before you failed.

7. **A problem only you have.** Mistaking a personal quirk for a market. "Scratch your own itch" requires that *enough others* share the itch.

## Investor Perspective

Investors are, in large part, judges of *problem quality* — because they know a great team on a poor problem usually loses:

- **They probe how painful the problem really is.** A seasoned investor will press: *how badly do people need this? Are they actively seeking a solution, or would you have to convince them they have a problem?* They're testing painkiller vs. vitamin. "People would love this" is a yellow flag; "people are already paying for terrible workarounds" is green.

- **They check that you started from the problem.** Investors are wary of solution-first founders who can describe their product in detail but get vague when asked about the *customer's actual pain.* A founder who deeply understands the problem — its frequency, cost, and current workarounds — signals they're building something real.

- **They recognize tarpits.** Experienced investors have watched dozens of teams die in the same seductive problems, so an idea that *looks* obvious can actually make them *more* skeptical, not less. They'll want to know why you'll succeed where many identical attempts failed.

The takeaway: lead with the *problem*, demonstrated by behavior (what people already do and pay), not the cleverness of your solution. Make the investor feel the pain is real and urgent — that's what makes them believe there's value to capture.

## Founder Perspective

For you, this chapter is a discipline to adopt before you write a single line of code:

- **Become a problem hunter first, a builder second.** Resist the urge to start building. Spend real time *finding and understanding a problem* — in your own life, your industry, the workarounds and complaints around you. The hunting is the work; the building comes after.

- **Hold every problem to the five tests and the painkiller bar.** Is it real, painful, frequent, expensive, widespread? Is the pain bad enough that people seek relief? If you can't honestly answer yes, keep hunting — a worse problem will not be saved by better execution.

- **Trust behavior, not words.** Look for what people *already do* about the problem — the money and time they spend, the workarounds they build. That's the truth; what they *say* is noise. (Chapters 13–14 give you the tools to gather this rigorously.)

- **Dig to the root.** When you find a promising problem, run the 5 Whys until you hit the specific, underlying pain. The root problem — not the surface symptom — is what you should build for.

- **Be honest about tarpits.** If your idea feels obvious and you suspect many have tried it, treat that as a reason for *more* scrutiny, not less. Find out why they failed before assuming you won't.

## Practical Exercises

1. **Problem inventory.** For one full day, write down every problem, frustration, or "I wish this were easier" moment you notice — in your own life and in others'. Don't judge them yet; just collect. You're training your problem-detector.

2. **Score five problems.** Take five problems from your inventory and score each on the five tests (real, painful, frequent, expensive, widespread). Which, if any, hit all five?

3. **Painkiller or vitamin?** For your top problem, decide honestly: is the pain "hair on fire" (people seek relief) or "nice to have" (you'd have to convince them)? What evidence supports your answer?

4. **Run the 5 Whys.** Take a surface complaint you've noticed and ask "why?" five times to dig toward the root problem. Write down the root. Is it more specific and more clearly valuable than the symptom you started with?

5. **Find the workaround.** Identify one problem where people currently use a clumsy manual workaround (a spreadsheet, a hack, a hired hand). What does the workaround tell you about the real problem — and about what a good solution must do?

6. **Tarpit check.** Take an idea you find appealing. Search for how many others have tried it and what happened. If many failed, write down *why* — and whether that reason applies to you.

## Summary

The first and highest-leverage move in building a startup is choosing the **problem** — and great startups begin with a *problem*, not a solution. Value comes from solving real problems, so a solution without a real problem creates nothing (Chapter 8's novelty trap). Starting from the problem also anchors you: you'll change your solution many times, but a real problem keeps every change pointed at the same valuable target.

Not all problems qualify. A problem **worth solving** is **real, painful, frequent, expensive, and widespread** — it must hit all five. The sharpest filter is the **painkiller vs. vitamin** distinction: painkillers solve urgent pain people actively seek to relieve (they sell themselves), while vitamins are nice-to-haves you must push on people who weren't looking. The "**hair-on-fire**" image captures the ideal — a problem so urgent the customer grabs any reasonable solution. On the **severity × frequency** matrix, the best problems sit in the *frequent + severe* corner, creating urgent, repeated, paying demand. And the most reliable signal of a real problem is **behavior, not words** — what people already *do* and *pay* (especially clumsy **workarounds**), since acknowledgment is cheap but motivation is what matters.

The best problems hide in your own frustrations, in industries you know, in workarounds and complaints, and in what new platforms have *newly made solvable.* When you find one, dig past the **symptom** to the **root problem** with the **5 Whys** — the root is where the specific, valuable, buildable problem lives. Finally, beware **tarpit ideas**: seductive, obvious, much-attempted problems that have killed countless startups for non-obvious reasons. Treat obviousness as a reason for more scrutiny, not less. Choose the problem well, and much of what looks like "execution difficulty" later simply dissolves.

## Key Takeaways

- **Start with the problem, not the solution** — the problem is the source of value; the solution just delivers it.
- A problem worth solving is **real, painful, frequent, expensive, and widespread** (all five).
- Aim for **painkillers** (urgent pain people seek to relieve), not **vitamins** (nice-to-haves you must push).
- The best problems are **frequent + severe** — urgent, repeated, paying demand.
- Trust **behavior over words**: what people already *do* and *pay* (especially **workarounds**) is the real signal.
- Problems hide in **your own frustrations, industries you know, workarounds, complaints, and what's newly possible**.
- Dig from **symptom to root** with the **5 Whys** — the root problem is the valuable, buildable one.
- Beware **tarpit ideas** — seductive, obvious problems that have killed many startups; scrutinize obviousness.
- Much of a startup's later "execution difficulty" is really the **difficulty of the problem it chose**.

## Glossary of New Terms

- **Problem (worth solving)** — A real, painful, frequent, expensive, and widespread difficulty people experience; the proper starting point for a startup.
- **Solution** — The product or method that addresses a problem; changeable, and only valuable insofar as the problem is real.
- **Painkiller** — A product that solves an urgent, painful problem people are actively trying to fix; sells itself.
- **Vitamin** — A nice-to-have product that solves no urgent pain; must be pushed on people.
- **Hair-on-fire problem** — A problem so urgent the sufferer would grab any reasonable solution immediately.
- **Severity × frequency matrix** — A tool for evaluating problems by how painful and how frequent they are; the best sit in the frequent + severe corner.
- **Willingness to pay** — Whether and how much people would actually pay to solve a problem; best judged by behavior, not words.
- **Workaround** — A clumsy, makeshift way people cope with an unsolved problem; a strong signal of real, painful, unmet need.
- **Symptom** — A surface-level complaint, often masking a deeper root problem.
- **Root problem** — The underlying cause beneath a symptom; usually the specific, valuable problem worth building for.
- **5 Whys** — Asking "why?" repeatedly to drill from a symptom to its root cause.
- **Tarpit idea** — A superficially appealing, much-attempted problem with hidden, fatal difficulties that trap and kill most startups that try it.
